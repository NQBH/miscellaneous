\section{About COBUILD}
When the 1st COBUILD dictionary was published in 1987, it revolutionized dictionaries for learners.

It was the 1st of the new generation of language reference materials that were based on actual evidence of how English was used, rather than lexicographer intuition.

%
Collins and the University of Birmingham, led by the linguist John Sinclair, developed an electronic corpus in 1980s, called the Collins Birmingham University International Language Database (COBUILD).

This corpus, which for several years was known as the Bank of English\textcopyright, became the largest collection of English data in the world.

COBUILD dictionary editors use the corpus to analyze the way that people really use the language.

%
The Collins Corpus now contains 4.5 billion words taken from websites, newspapers, magazines and books published around the world, and from spoken material from radio, TV and everyday conversations.

New data is added to the corpus every month, to help COBUILD editors identify new words, grammatical structures, and meanings from the moment they are 1st used.

%
All COBUILD language reference books are based on the information their editors find the Collins Corpus.

Because the corpus is so large, their editors can look at lots of examples of how people really use the language.

The data tells them how the language is used; the function of different structures; which words are used together; and how often these words and structures are used.

%
All of the examples in COBUILD language materials are examples of real English, taken from the corpus.

The examples have been carefully chosen to demonstrate typical grammatical patterns, typically vocabulary and typical contexts.

%
COBUILD English Grammar is no exception: Collins editors and researchers have been able to use this wealthy of information to establish a unique and full description of English grammar, and to track the development of certain grammatical structures over time.

%
The corpus lies at the heart of COBUILD, and you can be confident that COBUILD will show you what you need to know to be able to communicate easily and accurately in English.

%
To learn more about COBUILD and the Collins Corpus, go to \url{www.collinselt.com} and click on `COBUILD Reference'.

\section{Introduction}
This grammar is suitable for anyone who is interested in the English language and how it works in everyday current contexts.

It has been written mainly for advanced-level students and their teachers, but any serious learner will find it a valuable reference tool.

%
The information in this book is taken from a long and careful study of present-day English involving the analysis of the Collins Corpus - a corpus of many millions of words of speech and writing.

\subsection{A functional approach}
Most people who study and use a language are interested in how they can \textit{do things} with the language - how they can make meanings, get attention, influence people, and learn about the world.

They are interested in the grammatical structure of the language as a way of getting things done.

%
A grammar that puts together the patterns of the language and the things you can do with them is called a \textit{functional grammar}.

%
This is a functional grammar; that is to say, it is based on the important relation between structure and function.

%
Each chapter is built around a major function of language, e.g. \textit{describing people and things}, and \textit{reporting what someone said}.

Each of these functions is regularly expressed in English by particular structures.

E.g., describing people and things is usually expressed by adjectives, and reporting what people say or think typically involves a reporting verb e.g. \textit{say}, followed by a clause beginning with \textit{that} or a clause with quotation marks (` ') around it.

%
This grammar follows up each major statement (often called a \textit{rule} in other grammars) with a detailed description of the uses surrounding it - including any exceptions.

The scope of the original function may then be extended.

E.g., the basic, central function of reporting verbs (Chap. 7) is to state what someone has said.
\begin{example}
    \underline{He said} he would be back soon.
\end{example}
It can be easily be extended to include what someone has written.
\begin{example}
    \underline{His mother wrote} that he had finally arrived home.
\end{example}
Then it can be widened to include thoughts and feelings; these do not need to be expressed in words, but the reporting structure is very convenient.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The boys thought} he was dead.
\end{example}
From this we can see the reporting clause as a more general way of introducing another clause.

\subsection*{Examples}
All the examples in this book are taken from the Collins Corpus.

%
As ever, the corpus lies at the heart of each grammar point described, helping compilers to make confident and accurate decisions about different structures and usage.

Examples themselves remain close to the corpus, with minor changes made so that they are more accessible to the learner.

They are carefully chosen so as to illustrate typical patterns and collocations in real-life situations.

\subsection{Groups of words that behave in the same way}
As well as providing a wealth of illustrative examples, this book gives further information about the grammar of a large number of specific words.

The actual words and phrases that are regularly used in each structure are given in a series of lists.

In this way, the learner can get a good idea of how large or small a grammatical class is, and how many words a certain rule applies to.

%
Wherever there is good reason to do so, the words and phrases are grouped together in a list in a meaningful arrangement.

So, e.g., at [1.21], separate groups are set in a single list, including animals, fish, words ending in \textit{-craft}, and foreign words ending in \textit{-s}.

These all share the same feature, i.e. that they can be either singular or plural nouns without any change in form - \textit{moose, salmon, aircraft, corps}.

From a purely grammatical point of view, they could all go in a single alphabetical list; however from a teaching and learning point of view, it is helpful to have them further classified according to their meaning.

\subsection{`Be creative'}
Certain areas of English grammar are very flexible and \textit{productive}.

Some are well known, e.g. the fact that almost any noun in English can modify another noun.

E.g., the noun \textit{steam} can be used in, among others, the following combinations: \textit{steam bath, steam room, steam engine, steam iron, steam power}, and \textit{steam train}.

%
With this in mind, several `Be creative' features are included to encourage learners to use their imagination, and to be more confident about expressing themselves.

In such cases, rather than giving a definite rule, the authors prefer to give guidance so that the user can make individual choices with no serious risk of error.

By describing the language in this way,  give plenty of scope for creativity and innovation.

\subsection{Accessibility}
When using a grammar, it can be difficult to find the information that you want.

This is often the biggest single problem for users of grammars, and a good reason why grammars are often unpopular with learners.

This grammar makes a special effort to support the user.

%
The authors have aimed to use the most up-to-date and commonly used grammatical terminology throughout.

Technical terms have been used only where there is no obvious alternative.

A glossary of terms is provided and they are also all listed in the index.

%
There is a contents list at the beginning of the book, and there is an individual contents list at the beginning of each chapter.

Using these of the index, the user will be able to find the section or paragraph where a function is associated with a structure.

%
Throughout the book there are paragraph headings that show the topic of almost every paragraph, and there are frequent additional headings for each section of a chapter.

At the top of each page, there is another heading to guide the user.

\subsection{New developments in language}
The continued development of the corpus enables us to keep us with the ever-changing nature of language.

At COBUILD, the authorscontinually track and research the development of grammatical features, e.g.:
\begin{itemize}
    \item[(i)] the use of the progressive with so-called stative verbs (e.g. \textit{I'm loving every minute of it})
    \item[(ii)] the use of \textit{much} in affirmative unmodified statements (e.g. \textit{There was much debate})
    \item[(iii)] the spread of generic pronouns (e.g. \textit{You get some people who are very difficult})
    \item[(iv)] the use of \textit{like} in reporting structures (e.g. \textit{And I was like, `wow!'})
    \item[(v)] the use of all-purpose question tags in some varieties of spoken informal English (e.g. \textit{innit})
\end{itemize}
The results are often fascinating and surprising, and enable us to ensure that each new edition of the Grammar gives you a clear portrayal of real English as it is written and spoken today.

\subsection{The grammar of business \& academic English}
An extension of our functional approach has been to focus on 2 main contexts in which English is used as a lingua franca throughout the world - business and academic English.

%
As a result, 2 supplementary sections identify the principal areas of grammar that learners need to master if they wish to communicate effectively in business and academic contexts.

%
The section on the grammar of business English looks at typical structures used in such contexts as sharing information, negotiating, and giving presentations.

The academic English section covers such areas as explaining results, reviewing research, and reporting findings.

Extensive cross-referencing allows the user to refer back to the main text, where structures are discussed in greater detail.

%
The authors hope that you will enjoy learning about English grammar from a functional perspective - from exploring the wealth of real-world examples of current language, to understanding how certain structures work in business and academic contexts.

The authors hope, too, that as a result, you will gain the confidence to use English creatively and effectively in a wide range of everyday situations.

\section{How to use this Grammar}
The COBUILD English Grammar is designed to be used both for quick reference and for in-depth study.

\subsection{Organization of the main text}
The main text of the Grammar is divided into 10 chapters.

The 1st 2 chapters deal with the noun phrase, Chaps. 3--5 with the verb phrase.

Chap. 6 deals with adverbs and prepositions, Chap. 7 with reporting, Chap. 8 with joining words, phrases and clauses, and Chaps. 9--10 with continuous text.

%
Each chapter consists of a series of main topics and each topic is divided into sections.

Paragraphs in the chapter are numbered, so that Chap. 1 runs from \textbf{1.1} to \textbf{1.251}, Chap. 2 from \textbf{2.1} to \textbf{2.302} and so on.

This numbering system makes it easy for the user to refer to different but related points.

There are cross-references throughout the text, either pointing to the main place where a topic is dealt with or to another paragraph where more information is given.

%
Most paragraphs also have a heading, saying in a few words what it deals with, especially which grammatical structure is being explained.

Some paragraph headings do not show specifically what the paragraph deals with, but indicate information of a different kind.

These paragraphs have the headings `Be careful,' `Be Creative', and `Usage Note'.

%
\textbf{[!]} `Be Careful' highlights points where people often have problems with a particular grammatical feature of English, e.g. because it is a feature where English is different from many other languages.

%
$[\star]$ `Be Creative' indicates that the rule that has been mentioned can be applied in English to a very large number of words.

E.g., it is nearly always possible to make the \textit{-ing} participle of a verb into an adjective that is used in front of a noun.

By taking note of these features, you can use the rules that have been presented in a creative and original way, giving you greater freedom to express yourself in English.

`Be Creative' features are explained in greater detail in the Introduction.

%
\textbf{[i]} `Usage Note' gives information about the use of individual words or small groups of words.

This information is important but cannot be generalized into a grammatical rule.

The Usage Notes help you to understand points that are important for the understanding of particular words, rather than points that relate to large numbers of words.

%
The U.S. flag symbol highlights paragraphs containing information about typical American English usage.

%
The speech bubble symbol identifies paragraphs describing structures that are most commonly found in spoken English.

%
Most of the grammatical explanations are followed by examples showing how the structure is used.

These examples are all taken from the Collins Corpus, and show how the structures are used naturally in speech or writing.

The examples therefore give important information about typical use of a structure, the words it is frequently used with, and the contexts in which it is likely to occur.

%
Throughout the book, grammatical explanations are followed by lists of the words that typically illustrate that grammatical point.

E.g., in Chap. 3, the point is made that many verbs can be either transitive or intransitive with the same meaning.

This is followed by a list of verbs that are frequently used in this way.

%
The lists go beyond the actual examples of use that are given, to other words that behave in similar ways.

They show whether the point being made can be applied to a small number or a large number of words.

If the group is small, all members of it are given.

If the group is large, then the most frequently used words are given.

%
These lists can be used to help you increase your vocabulary and to check that you are using newly learned English words correctly.

\section{Glossary of grammatical terms}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{abstract noun}: a noun used to describe a quality, idea, or experience rather than something physical or concrete; e.g., \textit{joy, size, language}.
    
    Cf. \textbf{concrete noun}.
    \item \textbf{active}: used for describing verb phrases e.g. \textit{gives}, and \textit{has made}, where the subject is the person or thing doing the action or responsible for the action.
    
    Cf. \textbf{passive}.
    \item \textbf{adjectival clause}: another name for \textbf{relative clause}.
    \item \textbf{adjective}: a word used to tell yu more about a thing, e.g. its appearance, color, size, or other qualities; e.g., \textit{$\ldots$ a \underline{pretty blue} dress}.
    \item \textbf{adverb}: a word that gives more information about when, how, where, or in what circumstances something happens; e.g. \textit{quickly, now}.
    
    There are several different kinds of adverb; adverbs of degree, manner, place, time, duration, and frequency.
    
    There are also \textbf{focusing adverbs}.
    \item \textbf{adverbial}: a word or combination of words added to a clause to give more information about time, place, or manner.
    
    See also \textbf{sentence adverbial} and \textbf{sentence connector}.
    \item \textbf{adverb of degree}: an adverb indicating the amount or extent of a feeling or quality; e.g. \textit{extremely}.
    \item \textbf{adverb/adverbial of duration}: an adverb or adverbial indicating how long something lasts; e.g. \textit{briefly, for a long time}.
    \item \textbf{adverb/adverbial of frequency}: an adverb or adverbial indicating how often something happens; e.g. \textit{often, once a week}.
    \item \textbf{adverb of manner}: an adverb indicating the way in which something happens or is done; e.g. \textit{carefully}.
    \item \textbf{adverb of place}: an adverb that gives more information about position or direction; e.g. \textit{Move \underline{closer}}.
    \item \textbf{adverb particle}: an adverb used as part of a phrasal verb; e.g. \textit{hide \underline{out}, sit \underline{up}, turn \underline{round}}.
    \item \textbf{affirmative}: not containing a negative word.
    
    Also called \textbf{positive}.
    \item \textbf{agent}: another name for \textbf{performer}.
    \item \textbf{agreement}: the relationship between a subject and its verb, or between a number or determiner and its noun; e.g. \textit{I look/she looks$\ldots$ 1 bell/3 bells}.
    
    Also called \textbf{concord}.
    \item \textbf{apostrophe s}: an ending ('s) added to a noun to mark possession; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ \underline{Harriet's} daughter$\ldots$ \underline{the professor's} husband$\ldots$ \underline{the Managing Director's} secretary}.
    \item \textbf{article}: see \textbf{definite article}, \textbf{indefinite article}.
    \item \textbf{aspect}: the use of verb forms to show whether an action is continuing, repeated, or finished.
    \item \textbf{attributive}: used for describing the position of adjectives when they are used in front of a noun.
    
    Cf. \textbf{predicative}.
    \item \textbf{auxiliary verb}: 1 of the verbs \textit{be, have}, and \textit{do} when they are used with a main verb to make verb forms, negatives, questions, and so on.
    
    Also called \textbf{auxiliary}.
    
    \textbf{Modals} are also auxiliary verbs.
    \item \textbf{bare infinitive}: another name for \textbf{infinitive without to}.
    \item \textbf{base form}: the form of a verb that has no letters added to the end and is not a past form; e.g. \textit{walk, go, have, be}.
    
    The base form is the form you look up in a dictionary.
    \item \textbf{broad negative}: 1 of a small group of adverbs including \textit{barely} and \textit{seldom} which are used to make a statement almost negative; e.g. \textit{I \underline{barely} knew her}.
    \item \textbf{cardinal number}: a number used for counting; e.g. \textit{one, seven, nineteen}.
    \item \textbf{classifying adjective}: an adjective used to identity something as being of particular type; e.g. \textit{Indian, wooden, mental}.
    
    They do not have comparatives or superlatives.
    
    Cf. \textbf{qualitative adjective}.
    \item \textbf{clause}: a group of words containing a verb.
    
    See also \textbf{main clause} and \textbf{subordinate clause}.
    \item \textbf{clause of manner}: a subordinate clause that describes the way in which something is done, usually introduced with \textit{as} or \textit{like}; e.g. \textit{She talks \underline{like her mother used to}.}
    \item \textbf{collective noun}: a noun that refers to a group of people or things; e.g. \textit{committee, team}.
    \item \textbf{color adjective}: an adjective referring to a color; e.g. \textit{red, blue, scarlet}.
    \item \textbf{common noun}: a noun used to refer to a person, thing, or substance; e.g. \textit{sailor, computer, glass}.
    
    Cf. \textbf{proper noun}.
    \item \textbf{comparative}: an adjective or adverb with \textit{-er} on the end or \textit{more} in front of it; e.g. \textit{friendlier, more important, more carefully}.
    \item \textbf{complement}: a noun phrase or adjective that comes after a linking verb e.g. \textit{be}, and gives more information about the subject or object of the clause; e.g. \textit{She is \underline{a} \underline{teacher}, She is \underline{tired}, They made her \underline{chairperson}}.
    \item \textbf{complex sentence}: a sentence consisting of 2 or more main clauses linked by a subordinating conjunction; e.g. \textit{We went inside when it started to rain}.
    \item \textbf{compound}: a combination of 2 or more words functioning as a unit.
    
    E.g., \textit{self-centered} and \textit{free-style} are compound adjectives, \textit{bus stop} and \textit{state of affairs} are compounds nouns, and \textit{dry-clean} and \textit{roller-skate} are compound verbs.
    \item \textbf{compound sentence}: a sentence consisting of 2 or more main clauses linked by a coordinating conjunction; e.g. \textit{They picked her up and took her into the house}.
    \item \textbf{concessive clause}: a subordinate clause, usually introduced by \textit{although} or \textit{while}, that contrasts with a main clause; e.g. \textit{\underline{Although I like her}, I find her hard to talk to}.
    \item \textbf{concord}: another name for \textbf{agreement}.
    \item \textbf{concrete noun}: a noun that refers to something we can touch or see; e.g. \textit{table, dress, flower}.
    
    Cf. \textbf{abstract noun}.
    \item \textbf{conditional clause}: a subordinate clause usually starting with \textit{if}.
    
    The event described in the main clause depends on the condition described in the subordinate clause; e.g. \textit{\underline{If it rains}, we'll go to the cinema}.
        
    \textit{They would be rich \underline{if they had taken my advice}}.
    \item \textbf{conjunction}: a word linking together 2 clauses, phrases, or words.
    
    There are 2 types of conjunction - \textbf{coordinating conjunctions}, which link parts of a sentence of the same grammatical type (\textit{and, but, or}), and \textbf{subordinating conjunctions}, which begin subordinate clauses (\textit{although, when}).
    \item \textbf{continuous}: another name for \textbf{progressive}.
    \item \textbf{contraction}: a shortened form in which an auxiliary verb and \textit{not}, or a subject and an auxiliary verb, are joined together and functions as 1 word; e.g. \textit{aren't, she's}.
    \item \textbf{coordinate clause}: a clause that is connected to another clause with a coordinating conjunction e.g. \textit{and or but}; e.g. \textit{He fell \underline{and broke his leg}}.
    \item \textbf{coordinating conjunction}: a word e.g. \textit{and, but}, or \textit{or} which joins together 2 clauses, phrases, or words of the same grammatical type.
    \item \textbf{copula}: a name sometimes used to refer to the verb \textit{be}.
    
    In this grammar, the term \textbf{linking verb} is used.
    \item \textbf{countable noun}: a noun that can be singular or plural; e.g. \textit{dog/dogs, lemon/lemons, foot/feet}.
    
    Also called \textbf{count noun}.
    \item \textbf{declarative}: a clause in the declarative form has the subject followed by the verb.
    
    Most statements are made in the declarative form.
    
    Also called \textbf{indicative}.
    \item \textbf{defining non-finite clause}: a participle clause that is placed after a noun phrase to identify the person or thing you are talking about; e.g. \textit{The girl \underline{wearing the red hat}}.
    \item \textbf{defining relative clause}: a relative clause that identifies the person or thing that is being talked about; e.g. \textit{I wrote down everything \underline{that she said}}.
    \item \textbf{definite article}: the determiner `\textit{the}'.
    \item \textbf{delexical verb}: a verb that has very little meaning in itself and is used with an object that carries the main meaning of the structure.
    
    \textit{Give, have}, and \textit{take} are commonly used as delexical verbs; e.g. \textit{She \underline{gave} a small cry}.
    
    \textit{I've \underline{had} a bath}.
    \item \textbf{demonstrative}: 1 of the words \textit{this, that, these}, and \textit{those} used in front of a noun; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ \underline{this} woman$\ldots$ \underline{that} tree}.
    
    They are also used as pronouns; e.g. \textit{\underline{That} looks nice$\ldots$ \underline{This} is fun}.
    \item \textbf{dependent clause}: another name for \textbf{subordinate clause}.
    \item \textbf{definite determiner}: 1 of a groups of determiners including \textit{the, that} and \textit{your} which you use when the person you are speaking to understands which person or thing you are talking about; e.g. \textit{\underline{the} old man, \underline{my} ideas}.
    \item \textbf{determiner}: 1 of a group of words including \textit{the, a, some}, and \textit{my} which are used at the beginning of a noun phrase.
    \item \textbf{direct object}: a noun phrase referring to a person or thing affected by an action, in a sentence with an active verb; e.g. \textit{She wrote \underline{her name}$\ldots$ I shut \underline{the windows}}.
    \item \textbf{direct speech}: speech reported in the words actually spoken by someone, without any changes in tense, person, and so on.
    \item \textbf{ditransitive verb}: a verb e.g. \textit{give, take}, or \textit{sell} which can have both an indirect and a direct object; e.g. \textit{She gave me a kiss}.
    \item \textbf{dynamic verb}: a verb e.g. \textit{run, give} or \textit{slice} which describes an action.
    
    Cf. \textbf{stative verb}.
    \item \textbf{\textit{-ed} adjective}: an adjective that ends in \textit{-ed}, and usually has the same form as the \textit{-ed} participle of a verb, or is formed by adding \textit{-ed} to a noun; e.g. \textit{a \underline{worried} look$\ldots$ \underline{skilled} workers}.
    
    Adjectives that do not end in \textit{-ed} but have the same forms as irregular \textit{-ed} participles are also called \textit{-ed} adjectives; e.g. a \textit{broken} bone.
    \item \textbf{\textit{-ed} particle}: a verb form e.g. \textit{walked} or \textit{played}, which is used to make perfect forms and passives, or in some cases an adjective.
    
    Irregular participles e.g. \textit{given} and \textit{broken} are also called \textit{-ed} participles because they behave like regular \textit{-ed} participles.
    
    Also called \textbf{past participle}.
    \item \textbf{ellipsis}: when you leave out words because they are obvious from the context.
    \item \textbf{emphasizing adjective}: an adjective e.g. \textit{complete, utter} or \textit{total} which stresses how strongly you feel about something; e.g. \textit{I feel a \underline{complete} fool}.
    \item \textbf{ergative verb}: a verb that can be either transitive or intransitive in the same meaning.
    
    To use the verb intransitively, you use the object of the transitive verb as the subject of the intransitive verb; e.g. \textit{He had boiled \underline{a kettle}$\ldots$ \underline{The kettle} had boiled}.
    \item \textbf{exclamation}: a word or sentence spoken suddenly and loudly in order to express surprise, anger, and so on; e.g. \textit{Oh gosh!}
    \item \textbf{finite}: a finite verb is inflected according to person or tense rather than being an infinitive or a participle.
    \item \textbf{first person}: see \textbf{person}.
    \item \textbf{focusing adverb}: a sentence adverb that indicates the most relevant thing involved; e.g. \textit{only, mainly, especially}.
    \item \textbf{future}: the use of \textit{will} or \textit{shall} with the base form of the verb to refer to future events; e.g. \textit{She \underline{will come} tomorrow}.
    \item \textbf{future progressive}: the use of \textit{will be} or \textit{shall be} and an \textit{-ing} participle to refer to future events; e.g. \textit{She \underline{will be going} soon}.
    
    Also called \textbf{future continuous}.
    \item \textbf{future perfect}: the use of \textit{will have} or \textit{shall have} and an \textit{-ed} participle to refer to future events; e.g. \textit{I \underline{shall have finished} tomorrow}.
    \item \textbf{future perfect progressive}: the use of \textit{will} or \textit{shall} with \textit{have been} and an \textit{-ing} participle to refer to future events; e.g. \textit{I \underline{will have been walking} for 3 hours by then}.
    
    Also called \textbf{future perfect continuous}.
    \item \textbf{gender}: a grammatical term referring to the difference between masculine and feminine words e.g. \textit{he} and \textit{she}.
    \item \textbf{generic pronoun}: 1 of a group of pronouns including \textit{you} and \textit{they} which are used to refer to people in general.
    \item \textbf{gerund}: another name for \textbf{\textit{-ing} noun}.
    \item \textbf{gradable}: a gradable adjective can be used with a word e.g. \textit{very} to say that the person or thing referred to has more or less of a quantity; e.g. \textit{very boring, less helpful}.
    \item \textbf{idiom}: a group of 2 or more words with a meaning that cannot be understood by taking the meaning of each individual word; e.g. \textit{to kick the bucket, to run wild}.
    \item \textbf{\textit{if}-clause}: a \textbf{conditional clause}; or a clause used to report a \textit{yes/no}-question.
    \item \textbf{imperative}: a clause in the imperative has the base form of the verb without a subject, e.g. \textit{\underline{Come} here$\ldots$ \underline{Take} 2 tablets every 4 hours$\ldots$ \underline{Enjoy} yourself}.
    \item \textbf{impersonal}: \textbf{\textit{it}} is an impersonal subject when it is used to introduce a fact, or when it is used in a split sentence; e.g. \textit{\underline{It's} raining$\ldots$ \underline{It} was you who asked}.
    \item \textbf{indefinite article}: the determiners \textit{a} and \textit{an}.
    \item \textbf{indefinite determiner}: 1 of a group of determiners including \textit{a, many} and \textit{several} which you use to refer to someone or something of a particular type, without saying which person or thing you mean; e.g. \textit{\underline{an} old man, \underline{several} suggestions}.
    \item \textbf{indefinite place adverb}: 1 of a group of adverbs including \textit{anywhere} and \textit{somewhere} which are used to indicate position or location in a general or vague way.
    \item \textbf{indefinite pronoun}: 1 of a group of pronouns including \textit{someone} and \textit{anything} which are used to refer to a person or thing in a generaly way.
    \item \textbf{indicative}: another name for \textbf{declarative}.
    \item \textbf{indirect object}: a 2nd object used with a transitive verb to indicate who or what benefits from an action, or gets something as a result of it; e.g. \textit{She gave \underline{me} a rose}.
    \item \textbf{indirect question}: a way of asking a question that makes it sound more polite; e.g. \textit{Can you tell me where the bank is?} instead of \textit{Where is the bank?}
    \item \textbf{indirect speech}: another name for \textbf{reported speech}.
    \item \textbf{infinitive}: the base form of a verb.
    
    It is often used with \textit{to} in front of it; e.g. \textit{(to) take, (to) see, (to) bring}.
    \item \textbf{infinitive without to}: the infinitive form without \textit{to} in front of it, used with modals and certain other verbs; e.g. \textit{You must \underline{go}$\ldots$ Let me \underline{think}}.
    \item \textbf{inflection}: the variation in the form of a word to show differences in tense, number, case, and degree.
    \item \textbf{\textit{-ing} adjective}: an adjective that has the same form as the \textit{-ing} participle of a verb; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ a \underline{smiling} face$\ldots$ a \underline{winning} streak}.
    \item \textbf{\textit{-ing} participle}: a verb form ending in \textit{-ing} that is used to make verb forms, and as an adjective.
    
    Also called the \textbf{present participle}.
    \item \textbf{\textit{-ing} noun}: a noun that has the same form as the \textit{-ing} participle of a verb; e.g. \textit{\underline{Swimming} is good for you}.
    \item \textbf{interjection}: another name for \textbf{exclamation}.
    \item \textbf{interrogative adverb}: 1 of the adverbs \textit{how, when, where}, and \textit{why} when they are used to ask questions.
    \item \textbf{interrogative}: a clause in the interrogative form has part or all of the verb phrase in front of the subject.
    
    Most questions are asked in the interrogative form.
    \item \textbf{interrogative pronoun}: 1 of the pronouns \textit{who, whose, whom, what}, and \textit{which} when they are used to ask questions.
    \item \textbf{intransitive verb}: a verb that is used to talk about an action or event that only involves the subject and so does not have an object; e.g. \textit{She arrived$\ldots$ I was yawning}.
    \item \textbf{inversion}: changing the word order in a sentence, especially changing the order of the subject and the verb.
    \item \textbf{irregular}: not following the normal rules for inflection.
    
    An irregular verb has a past form and/or \textit{-ed} participle that is formed in a different way from the regular ending.
    \item \textbf{lexical verb}: another name for \textbf{main verb}.
    \item \textbf{linking verb}: a verb that links the subject and complement of a clause; e.g. \textit{be, become, seem, appear}.
    
    Also sometimes called \textbf{copula}.
    \item \textbf{main clause}: a clause that is not dependent on, or is not part of, another clause.
    \item \textbf{main verb}: any verb that is not an auxiliary verb.
    
    Also called \textbf{lexical verb}.
    \item \textbf{mass noun}: (in this grammar) a noun that is usually an uncountable noun, but that can be used as a countable noun when it refers to quantities or types of something; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ 2 \underline{sugars}$\ldots$ cough \underline{medicines}}.
    \item \textbf{measurement noun}: a noun that refers to a unit of size, volume, weight, speed, temperature, etc.: e.g. \textit{mile, liter, degree}.
    \item \textbf{modal}: an auxiliary verb that is used with a main verb to indicate a particular attitude, e.g. possibility, obligation, prediction, or deduction; e.g. \textit{can, could, may, might}.
    
    Also called \textit{modal auxiliary} or \textit{modal verb}.
    \item \textbf{modifier}: a word or group of words that come in front of a noun; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ a \underline{beautiful sunny} day$\ldots$ a \underline{psychology} conference}.
    \item \textbf{negative}: used for describing a sentence that uses a word like \textit{not, never}, or \textit{no one} to indicate the absence or opposite of something, or to say that something is not the case; e.g. \textit{I \underline{don't} know you$\ldots$ I'll \underline{never} forget}.
    
    The opposite is \textbf{affirmative}.
    \item \textbf{negative word}: a word e.g. \textit{never} and \textit{not} which expresses a negative meaning.
    \item \textbf{nominal relative clause}: a subordinate clause that functions as a noun and often begins with \textit{what} or \textit{whatever}; e.g. \textit{\underline{What he said} was true}.
    \item \textbf{nominal \textit{that}-clause}: a subordinate clause that functions as a noun and begins with \textit{that}; e.g. \textit{He showed \underline{that it was true}}.
    \item \textbf{non-defining relative clause}: a relative clause that gives more information about someone or something, but that is not needed to identify them; e.g. \textit{That's Mary, \underline{who was at university with me}}.
    
    Cf. \textbf{defining relative clause}.
    \item \textbf{non-finite}: the non-finite forms of a verb are the infinitive and participle forms; e.g. \textit{to take, taking, taken}.
    \item \textbf{noun}: a word that refers to people, things, and abstract ideas e.g. feelings and qualities; e.g. \textit{woman, Harry, guilt}.
    \item \textbf{noun phrase}: a group of words that acts as the subject, complement, or object of a clause, or as the object of a preposition.
    \item \textbf{noun modifier}: a noun used in front of another noun, as if it were an adjective; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ a \underline{car} door$\ldots$ a \underline{steel} works}.
    \item \textbf{number}: the way in which differences between singular and plural are shown; e.g. \textit{flower/flowers, that/those}.
    
    See also \textbf{cardinal number} and \textbf{ordinal number}.
    \item \textbf{object}: a noun phrase that refers to a person or thing, other than the subject, which is involved in or affected by the action of a verb.
    
    See also \textbf{direct object} and \textbf{indirect object}.
    
    Prepositions are also followed by objects.
    \item \textbf{object complement}: a word that is used to describe the object of a clause and that occurs with verbs e.g. \textit{make} and \textit{find}; e.g. \textit{It made me \underline{tired}$\ldots$ I found her \underline{asleep}}.
    \item \textbf{ordinal number}: a number that is used to indicate where something comes in an order or sequence; e.g. \textit{1st, 5th, 10th, 100th}.
    \item \textbf{participle}: a verb form used for making different tenses.
    
    See \textbf{\textit{-ed} participle} and \textbf{\textit{-ing} participle} for more details.
    \item \textbf{partitive}: a word that gives information about the amount of a particular thing; e.g. \textit{pint, loaf, portion}.
    \item \textbf{passive}: verb forms e.g. \textit{was given, were taken, had been made}, where the subject is the person or thing that is affected by the action.
    
    Cf. \textbf{active}.
    \item \textbf{past form}: the form of a verb, often ending in \textit{-ed}, that is used for the past simple.
    \item \textbf{past participle}: another name for \textbf{\textit{-ed} participle}.
    \item \textbf{past perfect}: the use of \textit{had} with an \textit{-ed} participle to refer to past events; e.g. \textit{She \underline{had finished}}.
    \item \textbf{past perfect progressive}: the use of \textit{had been} with an \textit{-ing} participle to refer to past events; e.g. \textit{He \underline{had been waiting} for hours}.
    
    Also called \textbf{past perfect continuous}.
    \item \textbf{past progressive}: the use of \textit{was} or \textit{were} with an \textit{-ing} participle, usually to refer to past events; e.g. \textit{They \underline{were worrying} about it yesterday}.
    
    Also called \textbf{past continuous}.
    \item \textbf{past simple}: the use of the past form of a verb to refer to past events; e.g. \textit{They \underline{waited}$\ldots$ It \underline{fell} over}.
    \item \textbf{past tense}: a tense used to describe actions or events that took place in the past.
    
    See \textbf{tense} for more details.
    \item \textbf{perfect form}: a verb form with \textit{have} and an \textit{-ed} participle; e.g. \textit{I \underline{have met} him$\ldots$ We \underline{had won}}.
    \item \textbf{performative verb}: a verb that states explicitly what action the speaker is performing when he or she uses it; e.g. \textit{apologize, resign, christen}.
    \item \textbf{performer}: the person or thing that is responsible for the action expressed by the verb; e.g. \textit{\underline{Mark} phoned$\ldots$ Our dinner was eaten by \underline{the dog}}.
    \item \textbf{person}: a term used to refer to the 3 classes of people who are involved in something that is said.
    
    They are the 1st person (the person speaking or writing), the 2nd person (the person being addressed), and the 3rd person (the person or things that are being talked about).
    \item \textbf{personal pronoun}: 1 of a group of pronouns including \textit{I, you}, and \textit{me} which are used to refer back to the people or things you are talking about.
    \item \textbf{phrasal verb}: a combination of a verb and an adverb and/or a preposition, which have a single meaning; e.g. \textit{back down, hand over, look after, look forward to}.
    \item \textbf{phrase}: a set of words that is smaller than a clause, and that is based around a particular word class: e.g., a verb phrase is based around a main verb, and can also contain auxiliary verbs.
    
    See also \textbf{noun phrase, verb phrase} and \textbf{prepositional phrase}.
    
    \textbf{Phrase} is also sometimes used to refer to any group of words.
    \item \textbf{plural}: the form used to refer to more than 1 person or thing; e.g. \textit{dogs, women}.
    \item \textbf{plural noun}: a noun that is only used in the plural form; e.g. \textit{trousers, scissors, vermin}.
    \item \textbf{possessive}: a structure used to show possession; e.g. \textit{your, Jerry's, mine}.
    \item \textbf{possessive determiner}: a determiner e.g. \textit{my, your}, and \textit{their}.
    
    Also called \textbf{possessive adjective}.
    \item \textbf{possessive pronoun}: 1 of the words \textit{mine, yours, hers, his, ours}, and \textit{theirs}.
    \item \textbf{postdeterminer}: a small group of adjectives used after a determiner and in front of other adjectives; e.g. \textit{certain, remaining}.
    \item \textbf{predeterminer}: a word that comes in front of a determiner; e.g. \textit{\underline{all} the boys$\ldots$ \underline{double} the trouble$\ldots$ \underline{such} a mess}.
    \item \textbf{predicative}: used for describing the position of adjectives when they are used after a linking verb e.g. `be'.
    
    Cf. \textbf{attributive}.
    \item \textbf{preposition}: a word e.g. \textit{by, with} or \textit{from}, which is usually followed by a noun phrase or an \textit{-ing} form.
    \item \textbf{prepositional phrase}: a structure consisting of a preposition and its object; e.g. \textit{on the table, by the sea}.
    \item \textbf{present participle}: another name for \textbf{\textit{-ing} participle}.
    \item \textbf{present progressive}: the use of the present simple of `be' with an \textit{-ing} participle to refer to present events; e.g. \textit{Things \underline{are improving}}.
    
    Also called \textbf{present continuous}.
    \item \textbf{present perfect}: the use of the present simple of \textit{have} with an \textit{-ed} participle to refer to past events that exist in the present; e.g. \textit{She \underline{has loved} him for 10 years}.
    \item \textbf{present perfect progressive}: the use of \textit{have been} and \textit{has been} with an \textit{-ing} participle to refer to past events that exist in the present; e.g. \textit{We \underline{have been sitting} here for hours}.
    
    Also called \textbf{present perfect continuous}.
    \item \textbf{present simple}: the use of the base form or the \textit{s} form of a verb, usually to refer to present events; e.g. \textit{I \underline{like} bananas$\ldots$ My sister \underline{hates} them}.
    \item \textbf{present tense}: a tense used to describe events taking place in the present, or situations that exist in the present.
    \item \textbf{progressive}: a verb form that contains a form of the verb `be' and an \textit{-ing} participle; e.g. \textit{She \underline{was laughing}$\ldots$ They \underline{had been playing} badminton}.
    
    Also called \textbf{continuous}.
    \item \textbf{pronoun}: a word used instead of a noun, when you do not want to name someone or something directly; e.g. \textit{it, you, none}.
    \item \textbf{proper noun}: a noun that refers to a particular person, place, or institution; e.g. \textit{Nigel, Edinburgh, Christmas}.
    
    Cf. \textbf{common noun}.
    \item \textbf{purpose clause}: a subordinate clause, usually introduced by \textit{in order to}, or \textit{so that};
    
    e.g. \textit{I came here \underline{in order to ask you out to dinner}}.
    \item \textbf{qualifier}: any word, phrase, or clause that comes after a noun phrase, and gives extra information to expand its meaning; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ a book \underline{with a blue cover} $\ldots$ the shop \underline{on the corner}}.
    \item \textbf{qualitative adjective}: an adjective that is used to indicate a quality, and is gradable; e.g. \textit{funny, intelligent, small}.
    
    Cf. \textbf{classifying adjective}.
    \item \textbf{quantity expression}: a phrase ending in \textit{of} that allows you to refer to a quantity of something without being precise about the exact amount; e.g. \textit{some of, a lot of, a little bit of}.
    \item \textbf{question}: a structure that typically has the verb in front of the subject and that is used to ask someone about something; e.g. \textit{Have you have any money?}
    
    Also called \textbf{interrogative}.
    \item \textbf{question tag}: a structure or word that is used at the end of a statement in order to form a question.
    \item \textbf{reason clause}: a subordinate clause, usually introduced by \textit{because, since}, or \textit{as}; e.g. \textit{\underline{Since you're here}, we'll start}.
    \item \textbf{reciprocal pronoun}: the pronouns \textit{each other} and \textit{one another}, used to show that 2 or more people do or feel the same thing; e.g. \textit{They loved \underline{each other}}.
    \item \textbf{reciprocal verb}: a verb that describes an action that involves people affecting each other in the same way with the same action; e.g. \textit{They \underline{met} in the street$\ldots$ He \underline{met} her yesterday}.
    \item \textbf{reflexive pronoun}: a pronoun ending in \textit{-self}, e.g. \textit{myself} or \textit{themselves}, which is used as the object of a verb when the person affected by an action is the same as the person doing it.
    \item \textbf{reflexive verb}: a verb that is typically used with a reflexive pronoun; e.g. \textit{enjoy yourself; pride yourself on}.
    \item \textbf{reflexive clause}: a subordinate clause that gives more information about someone or something mentioned in the main clause.
    
    See also \textbf{defining relative clause} and \textbf{non-defining relative clause}.
    \item \textbf{relative pronoun}: a \textit{wh}-word e.g. \textit{who} or \textit{which}, used to introduce a relative clause; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ the girl \underline{who} was carrying the bag}.
    \item \textbf{reported clause}: the part of a reporting structure that describes what someone has said; e.g. \textit{She said \underline{that I couldn't see her}}.
    \item \textbf{reported question}: a question that is reported using a reporting structure rather than the exact words used by the speaker.
    \item \textbf{reported speech}: speech that is reported using a reporting structure rather than the exact words used by the speaker.
    
    Also called \textbf{indirect speech}.
    \item \textbf{reporting clause}: a clause that contains a reporting verb, which is used to introduce what someone has said; e.g. \textit{\underline{They asked} if I could come}.
    \item \textbf{reporting verb}: a verb that describes what people say or think; e.g. \textit{suggest, say, wonder}.
    \item \textbf{reporting structure}: a structure that reports what someone has said by using a reported clause rather than repeating their exact words; e.g. \textit{She told me she'd be late}.
    \item \textbf{result clause}: a subordinate clause introduced by \textit{so that} which gives the result of something; e.g. \textit{The house was severely damaged, \underline{so that it is now uninhabitable}}.
    \item \textbf{rhetorical question}: a question that you use in order to make a comment rather than to obtain information; e.g. \textit{Oh, isn't it silly?}
    \item \textbf{second person}: see \textbf{person}.
    \item \textbf{semi-modal}: the verbs \textit{dare, need}, and \textit{used to} which behave rather like modals.
    \item \textbf{sentence}: a group of words that express a statement, question, or command.
    
    A sentence usually has a verb and a subject, and may consist of 1 clause, or 2 or more clauses.
    
    A sentence in writing has a capital letter at the beginning and a full-stop, question mark, or exclamation mark at the end.
    \item \textbf{sentence adverbial}: an adverbial that applies to the whole clause, rather than to part of it; e.g. \textit{We \underline{possibly} have to wait and see}.
    
    See also \textbf{sentence connector}.
    \item \textbf{sentence connector}: a sentence adverbial used to introduce a comment or reinforce what is said; e.g. \textit{moreover, besides}.
    \item \textbf{\textit{s} form}: the base form of a verb with \textit{s} on the end, used in the present simple.
    \item \textbf{simple sentence}: a sentence that contains only 1 clause.
    \item \textbf{singular}: the form used to refer to or talk about 1 person or thing; e.g. \textit{dog, woman}.
    
    Cf. \textbf{plural}.
    \item \textbf{singular noun}: a noun typically used in the singular form; e.g. \textit{sun, business}.
    \item \textbf{split infinitive}: the placing of a word between \textit{to} and the base form of a verb; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ to \underline{boldly} go where no man has gone before}.
    \item \textbf{split sentence}: a sentence in which emphasis is given to either the subject or the object by using a structure beginning with \textit{it, what}, or \textit{all}; e.g. \textit{\underline{It's} a hammer we need$\ldots$ \underline{What} we need is a hammer}.
    \item \textbf{stative verb}: a verb that describes a state ; e.g. \textit{be, live, know}.
    
    Cf. \textbf{dynamic verb}.
    \item \textbf{subject}: a noun phrase that usually comes before a verb, and agrees with the verb in person and number.
    
    In active sentences, the subject usually refers to the person or thing who dose the action expressed by the verb; e.g. \textit{\underline{We} were going shopping}.
    \item \textbf{subjunctive}: a verb form that is used in some languages to express attitudes e.g. wishing, hoping, and doubting.
    
    The subjunctive is not very common in English, and is used mainly in conditional clauses e.g. \textit{If I were you$\ldots$}
    \item \textbf{submodifying adverb}: an adverb that is used in front of an adjective or another adverb in order to strengthen or weaken its meaning; e.g. \textit{$\ldots$ \underline{very} interesting$\ldots$ \underline{quite} quickly}.
    \item \textbf{subordinate clause}: a clause that begins with a subordinating conjunction e.g. \textit{because} or \textit{while} and which must be used with a main clause.
    \item \textbf{subordinating conjunction}: a conjunction that begins a subordinate clause.
    \item \textbf{substitution}: the special use of pronouns and other words to replace part or all of a clause; e.g. \textit{`Are you going to the party?' -- `I hope \underline{so}'}.
    \item \textbf{superlative}: an adjective or adverb with \textit{-est} on the end or \textit{most} in front of it; e.g. \textit{thinnest, quickest, most wisely}.
    \item \textbf{tense}: the verb form that shows whether you are referring to the past or the present.
    \item \textbf{\textit{that}-clause}: a clause starting with `\textit{that}' which is used mainly when reporting what someone has said; e.g. \textit{She said \underline{that she'd wash up for me}}.
    
    \textit{That} can be omitted when the clause is used after a reporting verb.
    \item \textbf{third person}: see \textbf{person}.
    \item \textbf{time adverbial}: an adverbial that gives more information about when something happens; e.g. \textit{I saw her \underline{yesterday}}.
    \item \textbf{time clause}: a subordinate clause that indicates the time of an event; e.g. \textit{I'll phone you \underline{when I get back}}.
    \item \textbf{title}: a word used before a person's name to show their position or status; e.g. \textit{Mrs, Lord, Queen}.
    \item \textbf{\textit{to}-infinitive}: the base form of a verb preceded by \textit{to}; e.g. \textit{to go, to have, to jump}.
    \item \textbf{transitive verb}: a verb used to talk about an action or event that involves more than 1 person or thing, and so is followed by an object; e.g. \textit{She's \underline{wasting her money}}.
    \item \textbf{uncountable noun}: a noun that refers to a general kind of thing rather than to an individual item, and so has only 1 form; e.g. \textit{money, furniture, intelligence}.
    
    Also called \textbf{uncount noun}.
    \item \textbf{verb}: a word used with a subject to say what someone or something does, or what happens to them; e.g. \textit{sing, spill, die}.
    \item \textbf{verb phrase}: a main verb, or a main verb preceded by 1 or more auxiliary verbs, which combines with a subject to say what someone or something does, or what happens to them; e.g. \textit{\underline{I'll show} them$\ldots$ She\underline{'s been sick}}.
    \item \textbf{vocative}: a word used when speaking to someone, just as if it were their name; e.g. \textit{darling, madam}.
    \item \textbf{\textit{wh}-clause}: a clause starting with a \textit{wh}-word.
    \item \textbf{\textit{whether}-clause}: a clause used to report a \textit{yes/no}-question; e.g. \textit{I asked her \underline{whether she'd seen him}}.
    \item \textbf{\textit{wh}-question}: a question that expects an answer giving a particular person, place, thing, amount, and so on, rather than just \textit{yes} or \textit{no}.
    \item \textbf{\textit{wh}-word}: 1 of a group of words starting with \textit{wh-}, e.g. \textit{what, when} or \textit{who}, which are used in \textit{wh}-questions.
    
    \textit{How} is also called a \textit{wh}-word because it behaves like the other \textit{wh}-words.
    \item \textbf{\textit{yes/no}-question}: a question that can be answered simply with either \textit{yes} or \textit{no}; e.g. \textit{Would you like some more tea?}
\end{enumerate}

\chapter{Referring to people \& things: nouns, pronouns, \& determiners}

\section{Introduction to the noun phrase}
\fbox{1.1} At its simplest, language is used to talk about people and things.

People do this by using words in a variety of ways, e.g. to make statements, to ask questions, and to give orders.

The words chosen are arranged into groups, either around a noun or around a verb.

They are called \textbf{noun phrases} and \textbf{verb phrases}.

%
\textbf{Noun phrases} tell us which people or things are being talked about.

\textbf{Verb phrases} tell us what is being said about them, e.g. what they are doing.

%
Chaps. 1--2 of this grammar deal with noun phrases.

For information about verb phrases, see Chap. 3.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.2} A noun phrase can be the \textbf{subject} or \textbf{object} of a verb, it can follow a \textbf{linking verb}, or it can be the object of a \textbf{preposition}.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Babies} cry when they are hungry.
    
    I couldn't feel \underline{anger} against him.
    
    They were \underline{teachers}.
    
    Let us work together in \underline{peace}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.3} You use a noun phrase to talk about someone or something by naming them.

You do this by using a general name, called a \textbf{noun} or \textbf{common noun}, or by using a specific name, called a \textbf{proper noun}.

%
Proper nouns are mainly used for people, places, and events.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Mary} likes strawberries.
    
    I went to \underline{Drexel University} and then I went to \underline{Pittsburgh} to work for a psychiatrist.
    
    We flew to \underline{Genera} with \underline{British Airways}.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{1.52--1.58} for more information about \textbf{proper nouns}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.4} If you use a common noun, you are saying that the person or thing you are talking about can be put in a set with others that are similar in some way.

%
If you just want to say that the person or thing is in that set, you use an \textbf{indefinite determiner} with the common noun.
\begin{example}
    I met \underline{a girl} who was \underline{a student} there.
    
    Have you got \underline{any comment} to make about that?
    
    There are \underline{some diseases} that are clearly inherited.
\end{example}
If you want to show which member of a set you are talking about, you use a \textbf{definite determiner} with a common noun.
\begin{example}
    I put \underline{my arm} round \underline{her shoulders}.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{the destruction} of \underline{their city}.
    
    She came in to see me \underline{this morning}.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{1.162--1.251} for more information about \textbf{determiners}, and paragraphs \textbf{1.13--1.92} for more information about \textbf{nouns}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.5} You may decide not to name the person or thing and to use a \textbf{pronoun} rather than a proper noun or common noun.

%
You usually do this because the person or thing has already been named, so you refer to them by using a \textbf{personal pronoun} or a \textbf{demonstrative pronoun}.
\begin{example}
    Max will believe us, won't \underline{he}?
    
    `Could \underline{I} speak to Sue, please?' - `\underline{I}'m sorry, \underline{she} doesn't work here now.'
    
    Some people have servants to cook for \underline{them}.
    
    \underline{This} led to widespread criticism.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{1.95--1.106} for more information about \textbf{personal pronouns}, and paragraphs \textbf{1.124--1.127} for more information about \textbf{demonstrative pronouns}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.6} You may decide not to name the person or thing at all, e.g. because you do not want to, you think it is not important, you do not know, or you want to be vague or mysterious while telling a story.

In such cases you use an \textbf{indefinite pronoun}, which does not refer to any particular person or thing.
\begin{example}
    I had to say \underline{something}.
    
    In this country \underline{nobody} trusts \underline{anyone}.
    
    A moment later, his heart seemed to stop as he sensed the sudden movement of \underline{someone} behind him.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{1.128--1.141} for more information about \textbf{indefinite pronouns}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.7} If you want to give more information about the person or thing you are talking about, rather than just giving their general or specific name, you can use a \textbf{modifier} e.g. an \textbf{adjective}, or you can add extra information in the form of a phrase or a clause, for example.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.8} Most \textbf{adjectives} are used as modifiers.

Nouns are also often used as modifiers.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a \underline{big} city.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{blue} ink.
    
    He opened the car door.
    
    $\ldots$ the \underline{oil} industry.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{2.2--2.168} for more information about \textbf{adjectives}, and paragraphs \textbf{2.169--2.174} for more information about \textbf{noun modifiers}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.9} You can add a prepositional phrase, a relative clause, a relative clause, an adverb of place of time, or a \textit{to}-infinitive after the noun.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a girl \underline{in a dark grey dress}.
    
    $\ldots$ the man \underline{who employed me}.
    
    $\ldots$ the room \underline{upstairs}.
    
    $\ldots$ the desire \underline{to kill}.
\end{example}
Adjectives and participles are also sometimes used after the noun, usually in combination with other words.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ the Minister \underline{responsible for national security}.
    
    $\ldots$ the 3 cards \underline{lying on the table}.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{2.272--2.302} for more details about information that is added after the noun.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.10} In particular, prepositional phrases beginning with \textit{of} are very common, because they can express many different kinds of relationship between the 2 noun phrases.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ strong feelings of \underline{jealousy}.
    
    $\ldots$ a picture \underline{of a house}.
    
    $\ldots$ the rebuilding \underline{of the old hospital}.
    
    $\ldots$ the daughter \underline{of the village cobbler}.
    
    $\ldots$ problems \underline{of varying complexity}.
    
    $\ldots$ the arrival \underline{of the police}.
\end{example}
For more information about the use of \textit{of} in the noun phrase see paragraphs \textbf{2.277--2.283}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.11} If you want to refer separately to more than 1 person or thing, or you want to describe them in more than 1 way, you link noun phrases using the \textbf{conjunctions} \textit{and, or}, or \textit{but}.

Sometimes you use a common instead of \textit{and}, or just put 1 word next to another.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ \underline{a table and chair}.
    
    $\ldots$ his obligations with regard to \underline{Amanda, Robert and Matthew}.
    
    $\ldots$ some \underline{fruit or cheese} afterwards.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{her long black skirt}.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{8.171--8.201} for more information about the use of conjunctions to link noun phrases and words within noun phrases.

\noindent\fbox{1.12} If you want to say how many things you are talking about, or how much of something here is, you use \textbf{numbers} and \textbf{quantity expressions}.
\begin{example}
    Last year I worked \underline{7} days a week \underline{14} hours a day.
    
    She drinks \underline{lots of} coffee.
\end{example}
\textbf{Numbers} are dealt with in paragraphs \textbf{2.208--2.239}, and \textbf{quantity expressions} are dealt with in \textbf{2.175--2.193}.

\section{Identifying people \& things: nouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.13} A noun is used to identity a person or thing.

In this chapter 6 main types of noun are described.

They are classified according to whether they have a plural form, whether they need a determiner in front of them, and whether they occur with a singular verb or a plural verb when they are the subject of the verb.

%
The 6 types are:

\textbf{classification}: \textbf{example}: \textbf{comments}: \textbf{paragraph}
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{countable nouns}: a bird, birds: have plural, need determiner: \textbf{1.15--1.22}
    \item \textbf{uncountable nouns}: happiness equipment: no plural, usually no determiner: \textbf{1.23--1.33}
    \item \textbf{singular nouns}: the moon, a day: no plural, need determiner: \textbf{1.34--1.40}
    \item \textbf{plural nouns}: clothes scissors: no singular: \textbf{1.41--1.46}
    \item \textbf{collective nouns}: the public, the staff: either singular or plural verb: \textbf{1.47--1.51}
    \item \textbf{proper nouns}: Mary, London, The United Nations: start with capital letter: \textbf{1.52--1.58}
\end{enumerate}
Many nouns have a number of different meanings, and so can be, e.g., a countable noun for 1 meaning, an uncountable noun for another, and a singular noun for another.

%
There are few other groups of nouns with special features.

These are dealt with in paragraphs \textbf{1.59--1.92}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.14} Most nouns do not begin with a capital letter, unless they are used to start a sentence.

However, the following types of noun are always spelled with a capital letter:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{proper nouns} or names
    \begin{example}
        $\ldots$ my sister \underline{Elizabeth}.
        
        I love reading \underline{Shakespeare}.
        
        I'll be in the office on \underline{Monday}.
        
        I think he's gone to \underline{London}.
    \end{example}
    For more information on proper nouns, see paragraphs \textbf{1.52--1.58}.
    
    Proper nouns that are time expressions are dealt with a Chap. 4, and those that are place names in Chap. 6.
    \item nouns that identify people of a particular nationality, or languages
    \begin{example}
        Can you think of some typical problems experienced by \underline{Germans} learning \underline{English}?
    \end{example}
    \item nouns that are the name of a particular product
    \begin{example}
        He drives a \underline{Porsche}.
        
        Put a bit of \underline{Sellotape} across it.
    \end{example}
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Things that can be counted: countable nouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.15} Many nouns have 2 forms, the \textbf{singular} form, which is used to refer to 1 person or thing, and the \textbf{plural} form, which is used to refer to more than 1 person or thing.

%
These nouns refer to people or things that can be counted.

You can put numbers in front of them.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ \underline{book}$\ldots$ \underline{books}.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{day}$\ldots$ \underline{days}.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{3 brothers}.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{10 minutes}.
\end{example}
These nouns make up the largest group of nouns in English.

They are called \textbf{countable nouns}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.16} When you use the singular form of a countable noun as the subject of a verb, you use a singular verb.

When you use the plural form of a countable noun as the subject, you use a plural verb.
\begin{example}
    A \underline{dog likes} to eat far more meat than a human being.
    
    Bigger \underline{dogs cost} more.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.17} Countable nouns have a determiner in front of them when they are used in the singular.
\begin{example}
    He got into \underline{the car} and started \underline{the motor}.
    
    They left \underline{the house} to go for \underline{a walk} after lunch.
\end{example}
When you use the plural form of a countable noun to talk about something in general, you do not use a determiner.
\begin{example}
    They all live in big \underline{houses}.
    
    Most classrooms have \underline{computers}.
\end{example}
However, if you are specifying a particular instance of something, you need to use a determiner.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The houses} in our street are all identical.
    
    \underline{Our computers} can give you all the relevant details.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.18} Here is a list of some common countable nouns:
\begin{example}

\end{example}
Note that many of these nouns have some meanings in which they are uncountable nouns, but they are countable nouns in their commonest meanings.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.19} For most countable nouns the plural form has \textit{-s} at the end, which distinguishes it from the singular form.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ bed$\ldots$ \underline{beds}.
    
    $\ldots$ car$\ldots$ \underline{cars}.
\end{example}
Some countable nouns have other differences between the singular and plural forms.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ bus$\ldots$ \underline{buses}.
    
    $\ldots$ lady$\ldots$ \underline{ladies}.
    
    $\ldots$ calf$\ldots$ \underline{calves}.
    
    $\ldots$ man$\ldots$ \underline{men}.
    
    $\ldots$ mouse$\ldots$ \underline{mice}.
\end{example}
For full information about the plural forms of countable nouns, see the Reference section.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.20} Some countable nouns have the same form for both singular and plural.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a \underline{sheep}.
    
    $\ldots$ 9 \underline{sheep}.
\end{example}
Many of these nouns refer to animals or fish; others are more varied in meaning:
\begin{example}
    bison, deer, elk, greenfly, grouse, moose, reindeer, sheep
    
    cod, fish, goldfish, halibut, mullet, salmon, shellfish, trout, whitebait
    
    aircraft, hovercraft, spacecraft
    
    crossroads, dice, fruit, gallows, grapefruit, insignia, mews, offspring, series, species
    
    bourgeois, chassis, corps, patois, pr\'ecis, rendezvous
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.21} The names of many animals and birds have 2 forms, one singular and one plural.

However, when you are referring to them in the context of hunting or when you are saying that there are large numbers of them, it is quite common to use the form without \textit{-s}, even though you are referring to several animals or birds.
\begin{example}
    We went up north to hunt \underline{deer}.
\end{example}
Note that the plural form of the verb is used when several animals or birds are the subject of the sentence, even if you use the form without \textit{-s}.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Zebra are} a more difficult prey.
\end{example}
Similarly, when you are referring to a large number of trees or plants growing together, you can use the singular form of their name.

When you are referring to a small number or to individual trees or plants, you usually use the form with \textit{-s}.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Zebra are} a more difficult prey.
\end{example}
Similarly, when you are referring to a large number of trees or plants growing together, you can use the singular form of their name.

When you are referring to a small number or to individual trees or plants, you usually use the form with \textit{-s}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ the rows of \underline{willow} and \underline{cypress} which lined the creek.
    
    $\ldots$ the \underline{poplars} and \underline{willows} along the Peshawar Road.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.22} \textbf{[Be creative]} Although some names of animals, birds, trees, and plants are commonly used in the singular form with plural meaning, in fact all such names can be used in this way.

\subsection{Things not usually counted: uncountable nouns}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.23} Some nouns refer to general things e.g. qualities, substances, processes, and topics rather than to individual items or events.

These nouns have only 1 form, are not used with numbers, and are not usually used with the determiners \textit{the, a}, or \textit{an}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a boy or girl with \underline{intelligence}.
    
    Make sure everyone has enough \underline{food} and \underline{drink}.
    
    $\ldots$ new techniques in \underline{industry} and \underline{agriculture}.
    
    I talked with people about \underline{religion, death, marriage, money}, and \underline{happiness}.
\end{example}
These nouns are called \textbf{uncountable nouns}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.24} When you use an uncountable noun as the subject of a verb, you use a singular form of the verb.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Love makes} you do strange things.
    
    They believed that \underline{poverty was} a threat to world peace.
    
    \underline{Electricity is} potentially dangerous.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.25} Here is a list of some common uncountable nouns:
\begin{example}
    absence, access, age, agriculture, anger, atmosphere, beauty, behavior, cancer, capacity, childhood, china, comfort, concern, confidence, courage, death, democracy, depression, design, duty, earth, education, electricity, energy, environment, equipment, evil, existence, experience, failure, faith, fashion, fear, finance, fire, flesh, food, freedom, fun, ground, growth, happiness, health, help, history, ice, independence, industry, insurance, intelligence, joy, justice, labor, loneliness, love, luck, magic, marriage, mercy, music, nature, paper, patience, peace, philosophy, pleasure, policy, poverty, power, pride, protection, purity, rain, reality, refief, religion, respect, rice, safety, salt, sand, security, silence, sleep, strength, snow, spite, status, stuff, teaching, technology, time, trade, training, transport, travel, trust, truth, violence, waste, water, wealth, weather, welfare, wind, work, worth, youth
\end{example}
\textbf{[!] Be careful.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.26} There are some words that are uncountable nouns in English, but that refer to things that are considered countable in other languages.

%
Here is a list of the most common uncountable nouns of this type:
\begin{example}
    advice, baggage, furniture, hair, homework, information, knowledge, luggage, machinery, money, news, progress, research, spaghetti, traffic
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.27} Although uncountable nouns refer to things that cannot be counted and are not used with numbers, you often want to talk about an amount of something that is expressed by an uncountable noun.

%
Sometimes, you can do this by putting an \textbf{indefinite determiner} e.g. \textit{all, enough, little}, or \textit{some} in front of the noun.
\begin{example}
    Do you have \underline{enough} money?
    
    There's \underline{some} chocolate cake over there.
\end{example}
For more information on indefinite determiners that can be used with uncountable nouns, see paragraph \textbf{1.225}.

%
You can also put a \textbf{quantity expression} in front of the noun.

E.g., when you refer to water you can say \textit{drops of water, a cup of water, 4 gallons of water}, and so on.

%
The use of quantity expressions with uncountable nouns is explained in paragraphs \textbf{2.194--2.207}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.28} When you are sure that your reader or hearer will understand that a quantity of something is being referred to, you do not need to use a quantity expression.

%
E.g., in a restaurant you can ask for \textit{3 cups of coffee}, but you can also ask for \textit{3 coffees} because the person you are talking to will know that you mean \textit{3 cups of coffee}.

In this way, the uncountable noun \textit{coffee} has become countable.

%
Nouns used in this way are called \textbf{mass nouns}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.29} Mass nouns are often used to refer to quantities of a particular kind of food or drink.
\begin{example}
    We spent 2 hours talking over \underline{coffee} and biscuits in her study.
    
    We stopped for \underline{a coffee} at a small caf\'e.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.30} Similarly, some uncountable nouns can be mass nouns when they refer to types of something,

E.g., \textit{cheese} is usually an uncountable noun but you can talk about \textit{a large range of cheeses}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ plentiful cheap \underline{beer}.
    
    $\ldots$ profits from low-alcohol \underline{beers}.
    
    We were not allowed to buy \underline{wine} or spirits at lunch time.
    
    We shell a wide variety of \underline{wines} and liqueurs.
\end{example}
Mass nouns referring to different types of a substance are mainly used in technical contexts.

E.g. \textit{steel} is nearly always an uncountable noun, but in contexts where it is important to distinguish between different kinds of steel it can be a mass noun.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ imports of European \underline{steel}.
    
    $\ldots$ the use of small amounts of nitrogen in making certain \underline{steels}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.31} The following is a list of frequency used mass nouns:
\begin{example}
    adhesive, beer, brandy, bread, cake, cheese, claret, cloth, coal, coffee, cognac, coke, cotton, curry, deodorant, detergent, disinfectant, dye, fabric, fertilizer, fuel, fur, fin, glue, ink, insecticide, iron, jam, jelly, juice, lager, liqueur, lotion, material, meat, medicine, metal, milk, oil, ointment, ore, paint, perfume, pesticide, plastic, poison, preservative, ribbon, salad, sauce, sherry, soap, soil, soup, steel, sugar, tea, vodka, whisky, wine, wood, wool, yarn, yoghurt
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.32} There are also some other nouns that can be uncountable nouns when they refer to a thing in general, and countable nouns when they refer to a particular instance of it.

%
Some nouns are commonly both uncountable nouns and countable nouns.

E.g., \textit{victory} refers to the idea of winning in general but \textit{a victory} refers to a particular occasion when someone wins.
\begin{example}
    He worked long and hard and finally led his team to \underline{victory}.
    
    $\ldots$ his \underline{victory} in the Australian Grand Prix.
    
    Many parents were alarmed to find themselves in open \underline{conflict} with the church.
    
    Hundreds of people have died in ethnic \underline{conflicts}.
\end{example}
Some uncountable nouns are rarely or never countable nouns; i.e., they do not occur in a plural form, or with a number.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a collection of fine \underline{furniture}.
    
    We found Alan weeping with \underline{relief} and \underline{joy}.
    
    He saved \underline{money} by refusing to have a telephone.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.33} Some nouns that end in \textit{-s} and look as if they are plural are in fact uncountable nouns.

This means that when they are the subject of a verb, the verb is in the singular.

%
These nouns refer mainly to subjects to study, activities, games, and diseases.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Physics is} fun.
    
    \underline{Politics plays} a large part in village life.
    
    \underline{Economics is} the oldest of the social sciences.
    
    \underline{Darts is} a very competitive sport.
    
    \underline{Measles is} in most cases a relatively harmless disease.
\end{example}
Here are 3 lists of uncountable nouns ending in \textit{-s}.

%
These nouns refer to subjects of study and activities:
\begin{example}
    acoustics, aerobics, aerodynamics, aeronautics, athletics, classics, economics, electronics, genetics, gymnastics, linguistics, logistics, mathematics, mechanics, obstetrics, physics, politics, statistics, thermodynamics
\end{example}
Note that some of these nouns are occasionally used as plural nouns, especially when you are talking about a particular person's work or activities.
\begin{example}
    His \underline{politics are} clearly right-wing.
\end{example}
These nouns refer to games:
\begin{example}
    billiards, bowls, cards, darts, draughts, skittles, tiddlywinks
\end{example}
These nouns refer to diseases:
\begin{example}
    diabetes, measles, mumps, rabies, rickets, shingles
\end{example}

\subsection{When there is only 1 of something: singular nouns}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.34} There are certain things in the world that are unique.

There are other things that you almost always want to talk about one at a time.

This means that there are some nouns, or more often some meanings of nouns, for which only a singular form is used.

%
When a noun is used with such a meaning, it is called a \textbf{singular noun}.

Singular nouns are always used with a determiner, because they behave like the singular form of a countable noun.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.35} When you use a singular noun as the subject of a verb, you use a singular form of the verb.
\begin{example}
    The \underline{sun was shining}.
    
    The \underline{atmosphere is} very relaxed.
\end{example} 
%
\noindent\fbox{1.36} Some singular nouns refer to 1 specific thing and therefore are used with the.

Some of these nouns, in fact, refer to something of which there is only one in the world.
\begin{example}
    There were huge cracks in \underline{the ground}.
    
    \underline{The moon} had not yet reached my window.
    
    Burning tanks threw great spirals of smoke into \underline{the air}.
    
    He's always thinking about the \underline{the past} and worrying about \underline{the future}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.37} Other singular nouns can be used to refer to 1 thing only when it is obvious from the context what you are referring to.

E.g., if you are in Leeds and say \textit{I work at the university}, you will almost certainly mean Leeds University.

%
However, in the following examples it is not clear exactly who or what the singular noun refers to, because there is not enough context.
\begin{example}
    In many countries \underline{the market} is small numerically.
    
    Their company looks good only because \underline{the competition} looks bad.
    
    You've all missed \underline{the point}.
\end{example}
Unless it is made clear which goods or products are being talked about, it is not possible for the reader or listener to be sure which group of potential buyers \textit{the market} refers to.

Similarly it is not possible to know exactly which company or group of companies \textit{the competition} refers to.

In the last example, the speaker is presumably going to state what he or she thinks \textit{the point} is.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.38} There are some activities that you do not usually do more than once at a time.

The nouns that refer to them are usually the object of a verb, and are used with the determiner \textit{a}.

%
In this structure the verb has very little meaning and the noun carries most of the meaning of the whole structure.

For more information about these verb $+$ object idioms, see paragraphs \textbf{3.32--3.45}.
\begin{example}
    I went and had \underline{a wash}.
    
    Bruno gave it \underline{a try}.
\end{example}
Some singular nouns are used so regularly with a particular verb that they have become fixed phrases and are idiomatic.
\begin{example}
    I'd like very much for you to \underline{have a voice} in the decision.
    
    Isn't it time we \underline{made a move?}
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.39} There are 2 special kinds of structure in which a singular noun is used.

%
A singular noun is sometimes used with the determiner \textit{a} after a linking verb.

See paragraphs \textbf{3.126--3.181} for more information about \textbf{linking verbs}.
\begin{example}
    Decision-making is \underline{an art}.
    
    The quickest way was by using the car.
    
    It was \underline{a risk} but he decided it had to be taken.
    
    They were beginning to find Griffiths' visits rather \underline{a strain}.
\end{example}
A singular noun is sometimes used with the determiner \textit{the}, followed by a prepositional phrase beginning with \textit{of}.
\begin{example}
    Comedy is \underline{the art of} making people laugh.
    
    Old machines will be replaced by newer ones to reduce \underline{the risk of} breakdown.
    
    He collapsed under \underline{the strain of} a heavy workload.
\end{example}
This group includes nouns used metaphorically; see paragraph \textbf{1.64} for more details.

%
Some singular nouns are always used to refer to 1 particular quality or thing, but are rarely used alone; i.e., they need to be specified in some way by the use of supporting material.

They can be used with a number of different determiners.
\begin{example}
    There was \underline{a note of satisfaction} in his voice.
    
    Bessie covered the last 50 yards at \underline{a tremendous pace}.
    
    Simon allowed \underline{his pace} to slacken.
    
    She was simply incapable of behaving in \underline{a rational and considered manner}.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{their manner} of rearing their young.
\end{example}
Nouns that are rarely used alone without supporting material are discussed in detail in paragraphs \textbf{1.59--1.65}.

\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.40} Some nouns are used in the singular with a particular meaning only in an idiomatic phrase.

They have the appearance of singular nouns, but they are not used as freely as singular nouns.
\begin{example}
    What happens down there is \underline{none of my business}.
    
    \underline{It's a pity} I can't get to him.
\end{example}

\subsection{Referring to more than 1 thing: plural nouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.41} There are some things that are considered to be plural rather than singular, so some nouns have only a plural form.

E.g., you buy \textit{goods}, but not \textit{a good}.

These nouns are called \textbf{plural nouns}.

%
Other nouns have only a plural form when they are used with a particular meaning.

E.g., an official meeting between American and Russian leaders is usually referred to as \textit{talks} rather than as \textit{a talk}.

In these meanings, these nouns are also called \textbf{plural nouns}.
\begin{example}
    Union leaders met the company for wage \underline{talks} on Oct 9.
    
    It is inadvisable to sell \underline{goods} on a sale or return basis.
    
    Take care of your \underline{clothes}.
    
    The weather \underline{conditions} were the same.
    
    All \underline{proceeds} are going to charity.
    
    Employees can have meals on the \underline{premises}.
\end{example}
Note that some plural nouns do not end in \textit{-s}: e.g. \textit{clergy, police, poultry}, and \textit{vermin}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.42} When you use a plural noun as the subject of a verb, you use plural form of the verb.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Expenses} for attending meetings \underline{are} sometimes \underline{claimed}.
    
    The \underline{foundations were shaking}.
    
    \underline{Refreshments were} on sale in the caf\'e.
    
    Attempts were made where \underline{resources were} available.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.43} You do not usually use numbers in front of plural nouns.

You can, however, use some \textbf{indefinite determiners} e.g. \textit{some} or \textit{many}.

For more information about the indefinite determiners that can be used with plural nouns, see the section beginning at paragraph \textbf{1.223}.

%
Some plural nouns usually have a definite determiner in front of them, because they are specific; some never have a determiner at all, because they are very general; and some are rarely used alone without extra information in the form of a phrase or a clause, e.g., because they need supporting material.

%
The lists in the following 2 paragraphs contain some common plural nouns that are frequently used in 1 of these ways.

Many of them have other meanings in which they are countable nouns.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.44} Some plural nouns are most commonly used with \textit{the}.
\begin{example}
    Things are much worse when \underline{the rains} come.
    
    \underline{The authorities} are concerned that the cocaine may be part of an international drug racket.
    
    The coach tour of Gran Canaria was a wonderfully relaxing way to see \underline{the sights}.
\end{example}
Here is a list of plural nouns that are most commonly used with \textit{the}:
\begin{example}
    authorities, foundations, fruits, heavens, mains, odds, pictures, races, rains, sights, waters, wilds
\end{example}
Some plural nouns are most commonly used with a possessive determiner e.g. \textit{my} or \textit{his}:
\begin{example}
    It offended \underline{her feelings}.
    
    \underline{My travels} up the Dalmation coast began in Dubrovnik.
    
    This only added to \underline{his troubles}.
\end{example}
Here is a list of plural nouns that are most commonly used with a possessive determiner:
\begin{example}
    activities, attentions, feelings, likes, movements, reactions, terms, travels, troubles, wants
\end{example}
Some plural nouns are most commonly used without a determiner.
\begin{example}
    There were 1 or 2 cases where people returned \underline{goods}.
    
    There is only 1 applicant, which simplifies \underline{matters}.
    
    They treated us like \underline{vermin}.
\end{example}
Here is a list of plural nouns that are most commonly used without a determiner:
\begin{example}
    airs, appearances, events, expenses, figures, goods, matters, refreshments, riches, solids, talks, vermin
\end{example}
Some plural nouns can be used both with or without determiners.
\begin{example}
    The house was raided by \underline{police}.
    
    We call \underline{the police}.
    
    A luxury hotel was to be used as \underline{headquarters}.
    
    The city has been \underline{his headquarters} for 5 years.
    
    We didn't want it to dampen \underline{spirits} which were required to remain positive.
    
    The last few miles really lifted \underline{our spirits}.
\end{example}
Here is a list of plural nouns that can be used with or without a determiner:
\begin{example}
    arms, basics, brains, clergy, costs, directions, essentials, greens, grounds, handcuffs, headquarters, interests, looks, means, morals, papers, particulars, people, police, poultry, premises, proceeds, rates, resources, specifics, spirits, supplies, talks, thanks, tracks, troops, values
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.45} Some plural nouns are rarely used alone without a modifier, or some other form of extra information, because they need supporting material.
\begin{example}
    He doesn't tolerate \underline{bad manners}.
    
    Our country's \underline{coastal defences} need improving.
    
    $\ldots$ the hidden \underline{pressures of direct government funding}.
\end{example}
Here is a list of plural nouns that are rarely used alone without a modifier or some other extra information:
\begin{example}
    affairs, conditions, defences, demands, details, effects, forces, hopes, lines, manners, materials, matters, pressures, proportions, quarters, relations, remains, sands, services, thoughts, wastes, ways, words, works, writings
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.46} 2 special groups of nouns are usually plural: nouns referring to clothes and some other things that people wear, and nouns referring to tools and some other things that people use.

%
This is because some clothes and tools, e.g. \textit{trousers} and \textit{scissors}, are made up of 2 similar parts.
\begin{example}
    She wore brown \underline{trousers} and a green sweater.
    
    He took off his \underline{glasses}.
    
    $\ldots$ using the \underline{pliers} from the toolbox.
\end{example}
When you want to refer to these items in general, or to an unspecified number of them, you use the plural form with no determiner.
\begin{example}
    Never poke \underline{scissors} into a light bulb socket.
    
    The man was watching the train through \underline{binoculars}.
\end{example}
Here is a list of some plural nouns that refer to clothes and other things that people wear:
\begin{example}
    braces, briefs, cords, dungarees, glasses, jeans, jodhpurs, knickers, leggings, overalls, panties, pants, pyjamas, shorts, slacks, specs, spectacles, sunglasses, tights, trousers, trunks, underpants
\end{example}
Here is a list of plural nouns that refer to tools and other things that people use:
\begin{example}
    binoculars, clippers, compasses, dividers, nutcrackers, pincers, pliers, scales, scissors, secateurs, shears, tongs, tweezers
\end{example}
When you want to refer to a single piece of clothing or a single tool, you use \textit{some} or \textit{a pair of} in front of the noun.

You refer to more than 1 item by using a number or a quantity expression with \textit{pairs of}.
\begin{example}
    I got \underline{some scissors} out of the kitchen drawer.
    
    I went out to buy \underline{a pair of scissors}.
    
    He was wearing \underline{a pair of} old grey \underline{trousers}.
    
    Liza has \underline{3 pairs of jeans}.
\end{example}
You can also use \textit{a pair of} when you are talking about things e.g. gloves, shoes, and socks that typically occur in twos.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ \underline{a pair of} new \underline{gloves}.
\end{example} 
A possessive determiner e.g. \textit{my} can be used instead of \textit{a}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ his favorite \underline{pair of shoes}.
\end{example}
When you use \textit{a pair of} with a noun in the plural form, the verb is singular if it is in the same clause.

If the verb is in a following relative clause, it is usually plural.
\begin{example}
    It is likely that \underline{a} new \underline{pair of shoes brings} more happiness to a child than a new car brings to a grown-up.
    
    I always wear a pair of long pants underneath, or \underline{a pair of pyjamas is} just as good.
    
    He put on \underline{a pair of} brown \underline{shoes}, which \underline{were} waiting there for him.
    
    He wore \underline{a pair of earphones}, which \underline{were} plugged into a radio.
\end{example}
You use a plural pronoun after \textit{a pair of}.
\begin{example}
    She went to the wardrobe, chose \underline{a pair of shoes}, put \underline{them} on and leaned back in the chair.
    
    He brought out \underline{a pair of} dark \underline{glasses} and handed \underline{them} to Walker.
\end{example}

\subsection{Referring to groups: collective nouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.47} Some nouns in English refer to a group of people or things.

These nouns are called \textbf{collective nouns}.

They have only 1 form, but many collective nouns have other meanings in which they are countable nouns with 2 forms.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.48} When you use a collective noun, you can use either a singular verb or a plural verb after it.

%
You choose a singular verb if you think of the group as a single unit, and a plural verb if you think of the group as a number of individuals.
\begin{example}
    Our little \underline{group is} complete again.
    
    A 2nd \underline{group are} those parents who feel that we were too harsh.
    
    Our \underline{family isn't} poor any more.
    
    My \underline{family are} all perfectly normal.
    
    \underline{The enemy was moving} slowly to the east.
    
    \underline{The enemy were} visibly \underline{cracking}.
    
    His arguments were confined to books which \underline{the public was} unlikely to read.
    
    \underline{The public were deceived} by the newspapers.
\end{example}
\textbf{[AE]} In American English, it is more usual to use a singular verb unless the sentence contains an element that clearly refers to more than 1 person or thing.

%
The names of many organizations are collective nouns, and can be used with a singular or a plural verb.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The BBC is sending} him to Tuscany for the summer.
    
    \underline{The BBC are planning} to use the new satellite next month.
    
    \underline{England was leading} 18-0 at half-time.
    
    \underline{England are seeking} alternatives for their B team.
\end{example}
\textbf{[AE]} American English uses a singular verb for these.
\begin{example}
    \underline{GE reports} its 2nd-quarter financial results on Jul 16.
    
    \underline{New England is} going to sign him to a long-term contract.
\end{example}
If you want to refer back to a collective noun, you choose a singular pronoun or determiner if the previous verb is singular, and a plural pronoun or determiner if the previous verb is plural.
\begin{example}
    The government \underline{has said it} would wish to do this only if there was no alternative.
    
    The government \underline{have made up their} minds that \underline{they}'re going to win.
\end{example}
\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.49} Note that the words \textit{bacteria, data}, and \textit{media} are now often used as collective nouns, that is with either a singular or a plural verb and no change in form.

Some careful speakers think they should only be used with a plural verb because they have the rare singular forms \textit{bacterium, datum}, and \textit{medium} and are therefore countable nouns.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Medieval Arabic data show} that the length of the day has been increasing more slowly than expected.
    
    \underline{Our latest data shows} more firms are hoping to expand in the near future.
\end{example}
\textbf{[!] Be careful.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.50} Although you can use a plural verb after a collective noun, these nouns do not behave like the plural forms of countable nouns.

E.g., you cannot use numbers in front of them.

You cannot say \textit{3 enemy were killed}.

You have to say \textit{3 of the enemy were killed}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.51} Here is a list of common collective nouns:
\begin{example}
    aristocracy, army, audience, bacteria, brood, cast, committee, community, company, council, crew, data, enemy, family, flock, gang, government, group, herd, jury, media, navy, nobility, opposition, panel, press, proletariat, public, staff, team
\end{example}
Some collective nouns are also \textbf{partitives} (nouns that are used to talk about a quantity of something).

E.g., you talk about \textit{a flock of sheep} and \textit{a herd of cattle}.

See paragraph \textbf{2.198} for more information about these.

\subsection{Referring to people \& things by name: proper nouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.52} When you talk about a particular person, you can use their name.

Names are usually called \textbf{proper nouns}.

%
People's names are spelled with a capital letter, and do not have a determiner in front of them.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ Michael Hall$\ldots$ Jenny$\ldots$ Smith.
\end{example}
Ways of using people's names when you are speaking to them directly are explained in paragraph \textbf{9.95--9.99}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.53} Sometimes a person's name is used to refer to something they create.

You can refer to a painting, sculpture, or book by a particular person by using the person's name like a countable noun.

You still spell it with a capital letter.
\begin{example}
    In those days you could buy \underline{a Picasso} for \pounds300.
    
    I was looking at \underline{their Monets and Matisses}.
    
    I'm reading an \underline{an Agatha Christie} at the moment.
\end{example}
You can refer to music composed or performed by a particular person by using the person's name like an uncountable noun.
\begin{example}
    I remembered it when we were listening to \underline{the Mozart}.
    
    $\ldots$ instead of playing \underline{Chopin} and \underline{Stravinsky} all the time.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.54} Nouns that refer to relationships between the people in a family, e.g. \textit{mother, dad, aunt}, and \textit{grandpa}, can also be used like names to address people or refer to them.

They are then spelled with a capital letter.
\begin{example}
    I'm sure \underline{Mum} will be pleased.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.55} Words that show someone's social status or job are called \textbf{titles}.

They are spelled with a capital letter.

%
You use a title in front of a person's name, usually their surname or their full name, when you are talking about them in a fairly formal way or are showing respect to them.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ \underline{Doctor} Barker$\ldots$ \underline{Lord} Curzon$\ldots$ \underline{Captain} Jack Langtry$\ldots$ \underline{Mrs} Ford.
\end{example}
Here is a list of the most common titles that are used before names:
\begin{example}
    Admiral, Archbishop, Baron, Baroness, Bishop, Brother, Captain, Cardinal, Colonel, Congressman, Constable, Corporal, Dame, Doctor, Emperor, Father, General, Governor, Imam, Inspector, Justice, King, Lady, Lieutenant, Lord, Major, Miss, Mr, Mrs, Ms, Nurse, Police Constable, Pope, President, Prince, Princess, Private, Professor, Queen, Rabbi, Representative, Saint, Senator, Sergeant, Sir, Sister
\end{example}
A few titles, e.g. \textit{King, Queen, Prince, Princess, Sir}, and \textit{Lady}, can be followed just by the person's 1st name.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ Queen Elizabeth.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{Prince Charles}' eldest son.
    
    \underline{Sir Michael} has made it very clear indeed.
\end{example}
Ways of using titles when you are speaking to people directly are explained in paragraphs \textbf{9.97--9.98}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.56} Determiners, other modifiers, and phrases with \textit{of} are sometimes used with titles, and the person's name is omitted.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ Her Majesty the Queen and the Duke of Edinburgh.

    $\ldots$ the Archbishop of Canterbury.
    
    $\ldots$ the President of the United States.
    
    $\ldots$ the Bishop of Birmingham.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.57} Most words that are titles can also be countable nouns, usually without a capital letter.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ lawyers, scholars, poets, \underline{presidents} and so on.
    
    $\ldots$ a foreign \underline{prince}.
    
    Maybe he'll be a \underline{Prime Minister} 1 day.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.58} The names of organizations, institutions, ships, magazines, books, plays, paintings, and other unique things are also proper nouns and are spelled with capital letters.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ British Broadcasting Corporation
    
    $\ldots$ Birmingham University.
\end{example}
They are sometimes used with \textit{the} or another determiner.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ the United Nations $\ldots$ the Labor Party$\ldots$ the University of Birmingham$\ldots$ the Queen Mary$\ldots$ the Guardian$\ldots$ the Wall Street Journal$\ldots$ the British Broadcasting Corporation.
\end{example}
The determiner is not spelled with a capital letter, except in the names of books, plays, and paintings.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ The Grapes of Wrath.
    
    $\ldots$ A Midsummer Night's Dream.
\end{example}
Some time expressions are proper nouns, and are dealt with in Chap. 4.

\subsection{Nouns that are rarely used alone}
\noindent\fbox{1.59} There are some nouns that are rarely used alone.

They need extra material e.g. an adjective or a following phrase, because the meaning of the noun would not be clear without it.

Some of these nouns have many meanings; others have very little meaning on their own.

%
E.g., you cannot usually refer to someone as \textit{the head} without saying which organization they are head of.

Similarly, you cannot say that there was \textit{a note} in someone's voice without describing it as, e.g., \textit{a triumphant note} or \textit{a note of triumph}.

%
These nouns are used on their own only if it is obvious from the context what is meant.

E.g., if you have just mentioned a mountain and you say \textit{the top}, it is clear that you mean the top of that mountain.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.60} A \textbf{modifier} is an adjective or a noun that is added to a noun in order to give more information about it.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ her wide experience of \underline{political affairs}.
    
    I detected an \underline{apologetic note} in the agent's voice.
    
    He did not have \underline{British citizenship}.
    
    Check the \underline{water level}.
\end{example}
For more information on modifiers, see Chap. 2.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.61} Extra information after the noun is usually in the form of a phrase beginning with \textit{of}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ at the \underline{top of the hill}.
    
    There he saw for himself the \underline{extent of the danger}.
    
    Ever since the \underline{rise of industrialism}, education has concentrated on producing workers.
    
    $\ldots$ a high \underline{level of interest}.
\end{example}
For more information, see paragraphs \textbf{2.272--2.302}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.62} Some nouns are always used with a modifier.

E.g., you would not say that someone is \textit{an eater} because all people eat, but you may want to say that he or she is \textit{a meat eater} or \textit{a messy eater}.

%
Similarly, if you use \textit{range}, you have to refer to a particular \textit{price range} or \textit{age range}.

If you use \textit{wear} to mean \textit{clothing}, you have to say what sort of clothing, e.g. \textit{sports wear} or \textit{evening wear}.
\begin{example}
    Time was \underline{a slow eater}.
    
    $\ldots$ the other end of \underline{the age range}.
    
    The company has plans to expand \underline{its casual wear}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.63} Some nouns are almost always used with a possessive, that is a \textbf{possessive determiner}, \textbf{'s}, or a prepositional phrase beginning with \textit{of}, because you have to show who or what the thing you are talking about relates to or belongs to.
\begin{example}
    The company has grown rapidly since \underline{its formation} 10 years ago.
    
    Advance warning of \underline{the approach of enemies} was of the greatest importance.
    
    $\ldots$ the portrait of a man in \underline{his prime}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.64} Nouns that are being used metaphorically ($=$ when 1 thing is used to describe another thing) often have a modifier or some other form of extra information, often in the form of a phrase beginning with \textit{of}, to show what is really being referred to.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ \underline{the maze of politics}.
    
    He has been prepared to sacrifice this company on \underline{the altar of his own political ambitions}.
    
    He has worked out a scheme for \underline{an economic lifeline} by purchasing land.
    
    Lloyd's of London is \underline{the heart of the world's insurance industry}.
    
    $\ldots$ those on the lower rungs of \underline{the professional ladder}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.65} Many nouns have some meanings that need a modifier or some other form of extra information, and other meanings that do not.

%
Here is a list of these nouns:
\begin{example}
    affair, approach, area, back, band, base, bottom, boundary, branch, case, center, circumstances, citizenship, class, condition, crisis, culture, depth, development, discovery, eater, edge, edition, element, end, enterprise, epidemic, experience, extent, feeling, field, formation, fringe, ground, growth, head, height, impression, inception, kind, length, level, limit, line, matter, movement, nature, note, period, point, position, power, prime, range, rate, regime, relic, repertoire, rise, role, scale, side, sort, stage, status, structure, stuff, style, system, texture, theory, thought, time, tone, top, transfer, type, version, view, wave, way, wear, wing, world
\end{example}

\subsection{Adjectives used as nouns: \textit{the poor, the impossible}}
\noindent\fbox{1.66} When you want to talk about groups of people who share the same characteristic or equality, you can use \textit{the} $+$ adjective.

E.g., instead of saying \textit{poor people}, you can say \textit{the poor}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ the help that's given to \underline{the blind}.
    
    No effort is made to cater for the needs of \underline{the elderly}.
    
    $\ldots$ the task of rescuing \underline{the injured}.
    
    $\ldots$ men and women who would join the sad ranks of \underline{the unemployed}.
    
    Working with \underline{the young} is stimulating and full of surprises.
    
    $\ldots$ providing care for \underline{the sick, the aged, the workless} and \textit{the poor}.
\end{example}
Note that you never add \textit{-s} to the adjective, even though it always refers to more than 1 person.

\textbf{[$\star$] Be creative.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.67} Although some adjectives are commonly used in this way, in fact it is possible to use almost any adjective in this way.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.68} When the adjective being used as a noun is the subject of a verb, you use a plural form of the verb.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The rich have benefited} much more than the poor.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.69} If you want to talk about a more specific group of people, you put a \textbf{submodifying adverb} ($=$ an adverb that you put in front of an adjective to give more information about it) or another adjective in front of the headword.

For more information about submodifying adverbs, see paragraphs \textbf{2.140--2.168}.
\begin{example}
    In this anecdote, Ray shows his affection for \underline{the very old} and \underline{the very young}.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{the highly educated}.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{the urban poor}.
\end{example}
If you mention 2 groups, you sometimes omit \textit{the}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a study that compared the diets of \underline{rich and poor} in several nations.
    
    $\ldots$ to help break down the barriers between \underline{young and old}.
\end{example}
With a few words e.g. \textit{unemployed} and \textit{dead}, you can say how many people you are referring to by putting a number in front of them.
\begin{example}
    We estimate there are about \underline{300 dead}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.70} When you want to refer to the quality of something rather than to the thing itself, you can use the appropriate adjective with \textit{the}.
\begin{example}
    Don't you think that you're wanting \underline{the impossible}?
    
    He is still exploring the limits of \underline{the possible}.
    
    $\ldots$ a mix of \underline{the traditional} and \underline{the modern}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.71} All color adjectives can also be used as nouns.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ patches of \underline{blue}.
    
    $\ldots$ brilliant paintings in \underline{reds} and \underline{greens} and \underline{blues}.
\end{example}
Clothing of a particular color can be referred to simply by using the color adjective.
\begin{example}
    The men wore \underline{grey}.
    
    $\ldots$ the fat lady in \underline{black}.
\end{example}
\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.72} Nationality adjectives that end in \textit{-ch, -sh, -se}, or \textit{-ss} can be used in a similar way, unless there is a separate noun for the people.

E.g., French people are referred to as \textit{the French} but Polish people are referred to as \textit{Poles} or \textit{the Poles}.
\begin{example}
    For many years \underline{the Japanese} have dominated the market for Chinese porcelain.
    
    Britons are the biggest consumers of chocolate after \underline{the Swiss} and \underline{the Irish}.
\end{example}

\subsection{Nouns referring to males or females}
\noindent\fbox{1.73} English nouns are not masculine, feminine, or neuter in the way that nouns in some other languages are.

E.g., most names of jobs, e.g. \textit{teacher, doctor}, and \textit{writer}, are used for both men and women.

%
But some nouns refer only to males and others only to females.

%
E.g., some nouns indicating people's family relationships, e.g. \textit{father, brother}, and \textit{son}, and some nouns indicating people's jobs, e.g. \textit{waiter} and \textit{policeman}, are used only to refer to males.

%
In the same way \textit{mother, sister, daughter, waitress, actress}, and \textit{sportswoman} are used only to refer to females.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.74} Words that refer to women often end in \textit{-ess}, e.g. \textit{actress, waitress}, and \textit{hostress}.

Another ending is \textit{-woman}, as in \textit{policewoman} and \textit{sportswoman}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ his wife Susannah, a former \underline{air stewardess}.
    
    \underline{A policewoman} dragged me out of the crowd.
    
    Steph Burton was named \underline{sportswoman} of the year.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.75} Words ending in \textit{-man} are either used to refer only to men or to both men and women.

E.g., a \textit{postman} is a man, but a \textit{spokesman} can be a man or a woman.

%
Some people now use words ending in \textit{-person}, e.g. \textit{chairperson} and \textit{spokesperson}, instead of words ending in \textit{-man}, in order to avoid appearing to refer specifically to a man.

%
\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.76} Most names of animals are used to refer to both male and female animals, e.g. \textit{cat, elephant, horse, monkey}, and \textit{sheep}.

%
In some cases there are different words that refer specifically to male animals or female animals, e.g. a male horse is a \textit{stallion} and a female horse is a \textit{mare}.

%
In other cases the general name for the animal is also the specific word for males or females: \textit{dog} also refers more specifically to male dogs, \textit{duck} also refers more specifically to female ducks.

%
Many of these specific words are rarely used, or used mainly by people who have a special interest in animals, e.g. farmers or vets.

%
Here is a list of some common specific words for male and female animals:
\begin{example}
    stallion, mare
    
    bull, cow
    
    cock, hen
    
    dog, bitch
    
    drake, duck
    
    fox, vixen
    
    gander, goose
    
    lion, lioness
    
    ram, ewe
    
    buck, hind, stag, doe
    
    tiger, tigress
    
    boar, sow 
\end{example}

\subsection{Referring to activities \& processes: \textit{-ing} nouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.77} When you want to talk about an action, activity, or process in a general way, you can use a noun that has the same form as the \textit{-ing} participle of a verb.

%
These nouns are called different things in different grammars: gerunds, verbal nouns, or \textit{-ing} forms.

In this grammar they are referred to as \textbf{\textit{-ing} nouns}.

%
It is sometimes difficult to distinguish and \textbf{\textit{-ing} noun} from an \textbf{\textit{-ing} participle}, and it is usually not necessary to do so.

However, there are times when it is clearly a noun, e.g. when it is the subject of a verb, the object of a verb, or the object of a preposition.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Swimming} is a great sport.
    
    The emphasis was on \underline{teaching} rather than \underline{learning}.
    
    The \underline{closing} of so many mills left thousands unemployed.
    
    Some people have never done any public \underline{speaking}.
\end{example}
The spelling of \textit{-ing} nouns is explained in the Reference section.

The use of \textit{-ing} adjectives is explained in paragraphs \textbf{2.63--2.76}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.78} Because \textit{-ing} nouns refer to activities in a general way, they are usually \textbf{uncountable nouns}; i.e., they have only 1 form, cannot be used with numbers, and do not usually have a determiner in front of them.

%
For more information on uncountable nouns, see paragraphs \textbf{1.23--1.33}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.79} You often use an \textit{-ing} noun because it is the only noun form available for certain verbs, e.g. \textit{eat, hear, go, come}, and \textit{bless}.

Other verbs have related nouns that are not \textit{-ing} nouns: e.g. \textit{see} and \textit{sight}; \textit{arrive} and \textit{arrival}, \textit{depart} and \textit{departure}.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Eating} is an important part of a cruise holiday.
    
    $\ldots$ loss of \underline{hearing} in 1 ear.
    
    Only 6\% of children receive any further \underline{training} when they leave school.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.80} If you want to describe the action expressed by the noun, you use 1 or more adjectives or nouns in front of it.
\begin{example}
    He served a jail sentence for \underline{reckless driving}.
    
    The police need \underline{better training} in dealing with the mentally ill.
    
    He called for a national campaign against \underline{under-age drinking}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.81} A few \textit{-ing} nouns, mostly words for sporting or leisure activities, and much more common than their related verbs.

In some cases there is no verb, although it is always possible to invent one.

E.g., you are more likely to say \textit{We want caravanning around France} than \textit{We caravanned around France}.

%
Here is a list of the commonest of these nouns:
\begin{example}
    angling, boating, bowling, canoeing, caravanning, electioneering, hang-gliding, mountaineering, paragliding, shoplifting, shopping, sightseeing, skateboarding, snorkeling, snowboarding, surfing, weightlifting, window-shopping, windsurfing, yachting
\end{example}
Although these words are not always associated with a verb, most of them can be used as \textit{-ing} participles.
\begin{example}
    I spent the afternoon \underline{window-shopping} with Grandma.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.82} Some \textit{-ing} nouns that are related to verbs are \textbf{countable nouns}.

They generally refer to the result of an action or process, or to an individual instance of it.

Sometimes their meaning is not closely related to that of the verb.

%
Here is a list of the commonest of these nouns:
\begin{example}
    beginning, being, building, drawing, feeling, finding, hearing, meaning, meeting, offering, painting, saying, setting, showing, sitting, suffering, turning, warning
\end{example}
For more information on countable nouns, see paragraphs \textbf{1.15--1.22}.

\subsection{Compound nouns: \textit{car park, mother-in-law, breakdown}}
\noindent\fbox{1.83} A single noun is often not enough to refer clearly to a person or thing.

When this is the case, a \textbf{compound noun} can be used.

A compound noun is a fixed expression that is made from more than 1 word, and that behaves as a noun.
\begin{example}
    Some people write out a new \underline{address book} every January.
    
    How would one actually choose a small \underline{personal computer}?
    
    Where did you hide the \underline{can opener}?
    
    $\ldots$ a private \underline{swimming pool}.
\end{example}
Once it is clear what you are referring to, it is sometimes possible to use just the 2nd word of a 2-word compound noun.

E.g., after mentioning \textit{a swimming pool}, you can just refer to \textit{the pool}.

%
Most compound nouns consist of 2 words, but some consist of 3 or more words.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a vase of \underline{lily of the valley}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.84} Some compound nouns are written with hyphens instead of spaces between the words.
\begin{example}
    I'm looking forward to a \underline{lie-in} tomorrow.
    
    He's very good at \underline{problem-solving}.
    
    Judy's \underline{brother-in-law} lived with his family.
\end{example}
Some compound nouns, especially very frequent ones, are written as 1 word.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ patterned \underline{wallpaper}.
    
    They copied questions from the \underline{blackboard}.
\end{example}
In some cases, you can choose whether to write a compound noun with or without a hyphen, or with or without a space.

E.g., both \textit{air-conditioner} and \textit{air conditioner} are possible, and both \textit{postbox} and \textit{post box} are possible.

%
A few compound nouns that consist of more than 2 words are written partly with hyphens and partly with spaces, e.g. \textit{back-seat driver} and \textit{bring-and-buy sale}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ children from \underline{1-parent families}.
    
    $\ldots$ a \underline{Parent-Teacher Association}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.85} Compound nouns may be countable, uncountable, singular, or plural.

Here is a list of some common countable compound nouns:
\begin{example}
    address book, air conditioner, air raid, alarm clock, assembly line, baby-sitter, back-seat driver, bank account, bird of prey, book token, blood donor, bride-to-be, bring-and-buy sale, brother-in-law, burglar alarm, bus stop, can opener, car park, compact disc, contact lens, credit card, dining room, drawing pin, driving license, estate agent, fairytale, father-in-law, film star, fire engine, fork-lift truck, frying pan, guided missile, health center, heart attack, high school, human being, letter box, lily of the valley, mother-in-law, musical instrument, nervous breakdown, news bulletin, old hand, 1-parent family, package holiday, Parent-Teacher, Association, parking meter, pen-friend, personal computer, polar bear, police station, post office, rolling pin, sister-in-law, sleeping bag, swimming pool, T-shirt, tea bag, telephone number, traveler's cheque, washing machine, X-ray, youth hostel, zebra crossing.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.86} Here is a list of some common uncountable compound nouns:
\begin{example}
    air conditioning, air-traffic control, barbed wire, birth control, blood pressure, bubble bath, capital punishment, central heating, chewing gum, common sense, cotton wool, data processing, do-it-yourself, dry-cleaning, family planning, fancy dress, fast food, 1st aid, food poisoning, further education, general knowledge, hay fever, heart failure, higher education, hire purchase, income tax, junk food, law and order, lost property, mail order, mineral water, nail varnish, natural history, old age, pocket money, remote control, science fiction, show business, show jumping, sign language, social security, social work, soda water, stainless steel, table tennis, talcum powder, toilet paper, tracing paper, unemployment benefit, value added tax, washing powder, washing-up liquid, water-skiing, writing paper
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.87} Here is a list of some common singular compound nouns:
\begin{example}
    age of consent, arms race, brain drain, continental divide, cost of living, death penalty, diplomatic corps, dress circle, fire brigade, general public, generation gap, greenhouse effect, hard core, human race, labor force, labor market, long jump, mother tongue, open air, private sector, public sector, rank and file, solar system, sound barrier, space age, welfare state, women's movement
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.88} Here is a list of some common plural compound nouns:
\begin{example}
    armed forces, baked beans, civil rights, current affairs, French fries, grass roots, high heels, human rights, industrial relations, inverted commas, licensing laws, luxury goods, modern languages, natural resources, race relations, road works, social services, social studies, swimming trunks, vocal cords, winter sports, yellow pages
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.89} Most compound nouns consist of 2 nouns, or an adjective and a noun.
\begin{example}
    I listened with anticipation to the radio \underline{news bulletin}.
    
    $\ldots$ a big \underline{dining room}.
    
    \underline{Old age} is a time for reflection and slowing down.
\end{example}
however, some compound nouns are related to \textbf{phrasal verbs}.

These are sometimes written with a hyphen, and sometimes as 1 word.

They are rarely written as separate words.
\begin{example}
    The President was directly involved in the Watergate \underline{cover-up}.
    
    I think there's been a \underline{mix-up}.
    
    $\ldots$ a \underline{breakdown} of diplomatic relations.
    
    The singer is making a \underline{comeback}.
\end{example}
Here is a list of frequent nouns based on phrasal verbs.

They are shown in this list in the form in which they are most frequently written, either with a hyphen or as 1 word.
\begin{example}
    backup, bailout, blackout, breakaway, breakdown, break-in, breakout, break-up, build-up, buyout, check-in, checkout, check-up, comeback, cover-up, crackdown, cutbacks, drawback, feedback, follow-up, giveaway, handout, kick-off, lead-up, lookout, make-up, meltdown, mix-up, passer-by, run-in, runner-up, run-off, run-up, sell-out, setback, set-up, show-off, slowdown, takeaway, take-off, turnover, warm-up
\end{example}
For more information about phrasal verbs, see paragraphs \textbf{3.83--3.116}.

%
\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.90} In some cases, the meaning of a compound noun is not obvious from the words it consists of.

%
E.g., someone's \textit{mother tongue} is not the tongue of their mother but the language they learn as a child, and an \textit{old hand} is not a hand that is old but a person who is experienced at doing a particular job.

%
\textbf{[Bubble]} In other cases, the compound noun consists of words that do not occur on their own, e.g. \textit{hanky-panky, hodge-podge}, and \textit{argy-bargy}.

These nouns are usually used in informal conversation rather than formal writing.
\begin{example}
    Most of what he said was a load of \underline{hocus-pocus}.
    
    She is usually involved in some sort of \underline{jiggery-pokery}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.91} The plural forms of compound nouns vary according to the type of words that they consist of.

If the final word of a compound noun is a countable noun, the plural form of the countable noun is used when the compound noun is plural.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Air raids} were taking place every night.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{health centers}, banks, \underline{post offices}, and \underline{police stations}.
    
    Loud voices could be heard through \underline{letter boxes}.
    
    $\ldots$ the refusal of dockers to use \underline{fork-lift trucks}.
\end{example}
For full information about the plural forms of countable nouns, see the Reference section.

%
Compound nouns that are directly related to phrasal verbs usually have a plural form ending in \textit{-s}.
\begin{example}
    Nobody seems disturbed about \underline{cover-ups} when they are essential to the conduct of a war.
    
    Naturally, I think people who drive smarter, faster cars than mine are \underline{show-offs}.
\end{example}
A few compound nouns are less directly related to phrasal verbs, and consist of a countable noun and an adverb.

In these cases, the plural form of the countable noun is used before the adverb when the compound noun is plural.

%
E.g., the plural of \textit{runner-up} is \textit{runners-up}, and the plural of \textit{summing-up} is \textit{summings-up}.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Passers-by} helped the victim, who was unconscious.
\end{example}
Compound nouns that consist of 2 nouns linked by the prepositions \textit{of} or \textit{in}, or a noun followed by \textit{to-be}, have a plural form in which the 1st noun in the compound is plural.
\begin{example}
    I like \underline{birds of prey} and hawks particularly.
    
    She was treated with contempt by her \underline{sisters-in-law}.
    
    Most \underline{mothers-to-be} in their 40s opt for this test.
\end{example}
Some compound nouns have been borrowed from other languages, mainly French and Latin, and therefore do not have normal English plural forms.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Agents provocateurs} were sent to cause trouble.
    
    The \underline{nouveaux riches} of younger states are building palatial mansions for themselves.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.92} Compound nouns are fixed expressions.

However, nouns can always be used in front of other nouns in order to refer to something in a more specific way.

For the use of nouns as \textbf{modifiers}, see paragraphs \textbf{2.169--2.174}.

\section{Talking about people \& things without naming them: pronouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.93} When you use language, both in speech and writing, you constantly refer to things you have already mentioned or are about to mention.

%
You can do this by repeating the noun phrase, but unless there is a special reason to do so, you are more likely to use a \textbf{pronoun} instead.

%
Pronouns make statements less repetitive.
\begin{example}
    John took the book and opened \underline{it}.
    
    Deborah recognized the knife as \underline{hers}.
    
    Shilton was pleased with \underline{himself}.
    
    \underline{This} is a very busy place.
\end{example}
However, if you have mentioned 2 or more different things, you usually have to repeat the noun phrase to make it clear which thing you are now talking about.
\begin{example}
    Leaflets and scraps of papers were scattered all over the floor.
    
    I started to pick up \underline{the leaflets}.
    
    I could see a lorry and a car.
    
    \underline{The lorry} stopped.
\end{example}
For other ways of talking about things that have already been mentioned, see paragraphs \textbf{10.2--10.39}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.94} There are several different types of pronoun:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{personal pronouns}. See paragraphs \textbf{1.95--1.106}.
    \item \textbf{possessive pronouns.} See paragraphs \textbf{1.107--1.110}.
    \item \textbf{reflexive pronouns.} See paragraphs \textbf{1.111--1.118}.
    \item \textbf{generic pronouns.} See paragraphs \textbf{1.119--1.123}.
    \item \textbf{demonstrative pronouns.} See paragraphs \textbf{1.124--1.127}.
    \item \textbf{indefinite pronouns.} See paragraphs \textbf{1.128--1.141}.
    \item \textbf{reciprocal pronouns.} See paragraphs \textbf{1.142--1.145}.
    \item \textbf{relative pronouns.} See paragraphs \textbf{1.146--1.150}.
    \item \textbf{interrogative pronouns.} See paragraphs \textbf{1.151--1.153}.
\end{itemize}
There are a few other words that can be used as pronouns.

For more information about these, see paragraphs \textbf{1.154--1.161}.

\subsection{Talking about people \& things: personal pronouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.95} You use \textbf{personal pronouns} to refer to yourself, the people you are talking to, or the people or things you are talking about.

%
There are 2 sets of personal pronouns: subject pronouns and object pronouns.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.96} Subject pronouns refer to the subject of a clause.

%
Here is a table of subject pronouns:
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
        \hline
        & \textbf{singular} & \textbf{plural} \\
        \hline
        \textbf{1st person} & I & we \\
        \hline
        \textbf{2nd person} & you & you \\
        \hline
        \textbf{3rd person} & he, she, it & they \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.97} You refer to yourself by using the pronoun \textit{I}.

\textit{I} is always written with a capital letter.
\begin{example}
    \underline{I} don't know what to do.
    
    \underline{I} think \underline{I} made the wrong decision.
    
    May \underline{I} ask why Stephen's here?
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.98} You refer to the person or people you are talking to as \textit{you}.

Note that the same word is used for the singular and the plural.
\begin{example}
    \underline{You} may have to wait a bit.
    
    Would \underline{you} come and have a drink?
    
    How did \underline{you} get on?
\end{example}
\textit{You} is also used to refer to people in general, rather than to the person you are talking or writing to.

For more information on this, see paragraph \textbf{1.120}.

%
If you want to make it clear that you are addressing more than 1 person, you can create a plural form by using \textit{you} followed by a determiner, a number or a noun.

Forms like this can also be used in object position.
\begin{example}
    My granddad wants \underline{you both} to come round next Saturday.
    
    As \underline{you all} know, this is a challenge.
\end{example}
Here is a list of the most common plural forms of \textit{you}.
\begin{example}
    you all, you guys, you lot, you 2, you 3, you both
\end{example}
\textit{You guys} and \textit{you lot} are more frequent in informal English.

\textit{You lot} is not commonly used in American English.
\begin{example}
    I love working with \underline{you guys}.
    
    \underline{You guys} are great!
    
    So \underline{you lot} will have to look after yourselves.
\end{example}
Note that people often use \textit{you lot} when they are being very direct with a group of people, as in the last example above.

%
\textbf{[AE]} Some varieties and dialects of English have developed particular forms of plural \textit{you}.

In American English, particularly Southern American English, \textit{y'all} is sometimes used, especially in speech.
\begin{example}
    What did \underline{y'all eat for breakfast?}
    
    I want to thank \underline{y'all}.
\end{example}
In some dialects of British and American English, \textit{yous} and \textit{youse} are used as plural forms:
\begin{example}
    I know what some of \underline{yous} might be thinking.
\end{example}
Another plural form is \textit{you people}.

However, this can sound rude, and should only be used if you want to show that you are angry or annoyed with the people you are talking to.
\begin{example}
    `Why can't \underline{you people} leave me alone?' he says.
    
    `I can't work with \underline{you people},' Zoe said.
\end{example}
Some plural forms of \textit{you} - \textit{you guys, you lot, you 2, you 3} and \textit{y'all} - can be used as \textbf{vocatives}.

For more information on vocatives, see paragraphs \textbf{9.95--9.99}.

\textit{You all} and \textit{you both} cannot be used as vocatives.
\begin{example}
    `Listen, \underline{you guys},' she said. `I'll tell you everything you want.'
    
    Come on, \underline{you 2}. Let's go home.
    
    Bye, \underline{y'all}!
\end{example}
For more information on \textit{you} as an \textbf{object pronoun}, see paragraphs \textbf{1.104--1.106}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.99} You refer to a man or a boy as \textit{he}, and to a woman or a girl as \textit{she}.
\begin{example}
    My father is 78, and \underline{he}'s very healthy and independent.
    
    Billy Knight was a boxer, wasn't \underline{he}?
    
    Mary came in. \underline{She} was a good-looking woman.
    
    `Is Sue there?' - `I'm sorry, \underline{she} doesn't work here now.'
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.100} You use \textit{it} to refer to anything that is not male or female; e.g., an object, a place, an organization, or something abstract.
\begin{example}
    Have you seen Toy Story? \underline{It's} a good film for kids.
    
    `Have you been to London?' - `Yes, \underline{it} was very crowded.'
    
    How much would the company be worth if \underline{it} were sold?
    
    \underline{It} is not an idea that has much public support.
\end{example}
\textit{It} is often used to refer to an animal when its gender is not known or not considered to be important.

Some people also refer to babies in this way.
\begin{example}
    They punched the crocodile until \underline{it} let go of her.
    
    If the shark is still around \underline{it} will not escape.
    
    How Winifred loved the baby! And how Stephanie hated \underline{it}!
\end{example}
You also use \textit{it} in general statements, e.g. to refer to a situation, the time, the date, or the weather.
\begin{example}
    \underline{It} is very quiet here.
    
    \underline{It} is half past 3.
    
    \underline{It} is Jan 19th.
    
    \underline{It} is rainy and cold.
\end{example}
For more information on the use of \textit{it} in general statements, see paragraphs \textbf{9.31--9.45}.

%
Note that \textit{it} is also an \textbf{object pronoun}.

For more information on this, see paragraphs \textbf{1.104--1.106}.

%
\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.101} Although \textit{it} is used as a pronoun to refer to something that is not male or female, \textit{she} is sometimes used to refer to ships, cars, and countries.

Some people do not like this usage.
\begin{example}
    When the repairs had been done \underline{she} was a fine and beautiful ship.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.102} You use \textit{we} to refer to a group of people that includes yourself.

The group can be:
\begin{itemize}
    \item you can the person or people you are talking to
    \begin{example}
        Where shall \underline{we} meet, Sally?
    \end{example}
    \item you and the person or people you are talking to and 1 or more others not there at the time
    \begin{example}
        \underline{We} aren't exactly gossips, you and I and Watson.
    \end{example}
    \item you and 1 or more other people, but not including the person or people you are talking to
    \begin{example}
        I do the washing; he does the cooking; \underline{we} share the washing-up.
    \end{example}
    \item any group that you feel yourself to be part of, e.g. a school, your local community, or even mankind as a whole.
    
    For more information on this, see paragraph \textbf{1.122}.
\end{itemize}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.103} You use \textit{they} to refer to a group of things, or to a group of people not including yourself or the person or people you are talking to.
\begin{example}
    All the girls think he's great, don't \underline{they}?
    
    Newspapers reach me on the day after \underline{they} are published.
    
    Winters here vary as \underline{they} do elsewhere.
\end{example}
\textit{They} is also used to refer to people in general.

For more information on this, see paragraph \textbf{1.123}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.104} Object pronouns refer to the same sets of people or things as the corresponding subject pronouns.

%
Here is a table of object pronouns:
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
        \hline
        & \textbf{singular} & \textbf{plural} \\
        \hline
        \textbf{1st person} & me & us \\
        \hline
        \textbf{2nd person} & you & you \\
        \hline
        \textbf{3rd person} & him, her, it & them \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.105} Object pronouns are used as the object of a clause.
\begin{example}
    The nurse washed \underline{me} with cold water.
    
    He likes \underline{you}; he said so.
    
    The man went up to the cat and started stroking \underline{it}.
\end{example}
They can be the indirect object of a clause.
\begin{example}
    Send \underline{us} card so we'll know where you are.
    
    A man gave \underline{him} a car.
    
    You have to offer \underline{them} some kind of incentive.
\end{example}
They can also be the object of a preposition.
\begin{example}
    She must have felt intimidated by \underline{me}.
    
    Madeleine, I want to talk to \underline{you} immediately.
    
    We were all sitting in a caf\'e with \underline{him}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.106} Object pronouns can also be used after \textbf{linking verbs}.

E.g., you can say \textit{It was me, It's her}.

However, in formal or written English, people sometimes use a subject pronoun after a linking verb.

E.g., \textit{It was I, It is she}.

%
For more information on linking verbs, see paragraphs \textbf{3.126--3.181}.

\subsection{Talking about possession: possessive pronouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.107} When you are talking about people or things, you often want to say how they are connected with each other.

There are several different ways in which you can do this, but you usually do it by using a \textbf{possessive pronoun} to show that something belongs to someone or is associated with them.

%
Here is a table showing possessive pronouns:
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
        \hline
        & \textbf{singular} & \textbf{plural} \\
        \hline
        \textbf{1st person} & mine & ours \\
        \hline
        \textbf{2nd person} & yours & yours \\
        \hline
        \textbf{3rd person} & his, hers & theirs \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}
Note that \textit{its} cannot be used as a possessive pronoun.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.108} You use possessive pronouns when you are talking about the same type of thing that has just been mentioned but want to show that it belongs to someone else.

%
E.g., in the sentence \textit{Jane showed them her passport, then Richard showed them his}, \textit{his} refers to a passport and shows that it belongs to Richard.

%
Possessive pronouns are often used to contrast 2 things of the same type that belong to or are associated with different people.

E.g., \textit{Sarah's house is much bigger than ours}.
\begin{example}
    Her parents were in Malaya, and so were \underline{mine}.
    
    He smiled at her and laid his hand on \underline{hers}.
    
    Is that coffee \underline{yours} or \underline{mine}?
    
    My marks were higher than \underline{his}.
    
    Fred put his profits in the bank, while Julia spent \underline{hers} on a car.
    
    That's the difference between his ideas and \underline{ours}.
    
    It was his fault, not \underline{theirs}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.109} Possessive pronouns are used with \textit{of} to add information to a noun phrase.

This structure suggests that you are talking about 1 of a group of things.

%
E.g., if you say \textit{a friend of mine} you are talking about 1 of a number of friends whereas if you say \textit{my friend} you are talking about 1 friend in particular.
\begin{example}
    He was an old friend \underline{of mine}.
    
    A student \underline{of yours} has just been to see me.
    
    David Lodge? I've just read a novel \underline{of his}.
    
    It was hinted to him by some friends \underline{of hers}.
    
    The room was not a favorite \underline{of theirs}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.110} For other ways of showing that something belongs to someone or is connected with them, see paragraphs \textbf{1.211--1.221}.

\subsection{Referring back to the subject: reflexive pronouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.111} When you want to show that the object or the indirect object of a verb is the same person or thing as the subject of the verb, you use a \textbf{reflexive pronoun}.

%
Some verbs are very frequently used with reflexive pronouns.

For information about these, see paragraphs \textbf{3.26--3.31}.

%
Here is a table of reflexive pronouns:
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
        \hline
        & \textbf{singular} & \textbf{plural} \\
        \hline
        \textbf{1st person} & myself & ourselves \\
        \hline
        \textbf{2nd person} & yourself & yourselves \\
        \hline
        \textbf{3rd person} & himself, herself, itself & themselves \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}
%
\textbf{[!] Be careful.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.112} Unlike personal pronouns and possessive pronouns, there are 2 forms of the reflexive pronoun used for the 2nd person.

You use \textit{yourself} when you are talking to 1 person.

You use \textit{yourselves} when you are talking to more than 1 person, or referring to a group that includes the person you are talking to.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.113} You use a reflexive pronoun to make it clear that the object of a verb is the same person or thing as the subject of the verb, or to emphasize this.

%
E.g., \textit{John killed himself} means that John did the killing, and he was also the person who was killed.
\begin{example}
    He forced \underline{himself} to remain absolutely still.
    
    She stretched \underline{herself} out on the sofa.
    
    I'm sure history repeats \underline{itself}.
    
    We all shook hands and introduced \underline{ourselves}.
    
    The boys formed \underline{themselves} into a line.
    
    Here is the question you have to ask \underline{yourselves}.
\end{example}
You also use reflexive pronouns to show or emphasize that the indirect object of a verb is the same person or thing as the subject of the verb.

E.g., in the sentence \textit{Ann poured herself a drink}, Ann did the pouring and she was also the person who the drink was for.
\begin{example}
    Here's the money, you can go and buy \underline{yourself} a watch.
\end{example}
%
\textbf{[!] Be careful.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.114} Reflexive pronouns are not usually used with actions that people normally do to themselves, e.g. washing, dressing, or shaving.

So you do not usually say \textit{He shaves himself every morning}.

%
You can, however, sometimes use reflexive pronouns with these actions for emphasis, or to talk about a surprising event, e.g. a child doing something that they were not previously able to do.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.115} If the subject of a clause and the object of a preposition refer to the same person, and the clause does not have a direct object, you use a reflexive pronoun after the preposition.
\begin{example}
    I was thoroughly ashamed of \underline{myself}.
    
    Barbara stared at \underline{herself} in the mirror.
    
    We think of \underline{ourselves} as members of the local community.
    
    They can't cook for \underline{themselves}.
\end{example}
However, if the clause does have a direct object, you usually use a personal pronoun after the preposition.
\begin{example}
    I will take it home with \underline{me}.
    
    They put the book between \underline{them} on the kitchen table.
    
    I shivered and drew the rug around \underline{me}.
    
    Mrs Bixby went out, slamming the door behind \underline{her}.
\end{example}
Note that if the clause has a direct object and it is not obvious that the subject of the clause and the object of the preposition refer to the same person, you use a reflexive pronoun.

E.g., \textit{The Managing Director gave the biggest pay rise to himself}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.116} Especially in speech, people sometimes use reflexive pronouns rather than personal pronouns as the object of a preposition, in order to emphasize them.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ people like \underline{myself} who are politically active.
    
    $\ldots$ the following conversation between \underline{myself} and a 15-year-old girl.
    
    The circle spread to include \underline{himself} and Ferdinand.
    
    People like \underline{yourself} still find new things to say about Shakespeare.
    
    There is always someone worse off than \underline{yourself}.
    
    With the exception of a few Algerians and \underline{ourselves}, everyone spoke Spanish.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.117} You can use reflexive pronouns in addition to nouns to personal pronouns.

You usually do this in order to make it clear or to emphasize who or what you are referring to.
\begin{example}
    \underline{I myself} sometimes say things I don't mean.
    
    Sally \underline{herself} came back.
\end{example}
You also use a reflexive pronoun to compare or contrast 1 person or thing with another.
\begin{example}
    His friend looked as miserable as he felt \underline{himself}.
    
    It is not Des Moines I miss, but Iowa \underline{itself}.
\end{example}
The reflexive pronoun follows the noun or pronoun that it relates to.
\begin{example}
    It is hot in London; but I \underline{myself} can work better when it's hot.
    
    The town \underline{itself} was so small that it didn't have a priest.
    
    The lane ran right up to the wood \underline{itself}.
\end{example}
It can also be placed at the end of the clause.
\begin{example}
    I am not a particularly punctual person \underline{myself}.
    
    You'll probably understand better when you are a grandparent \underline{yourself}.
    
    It is rare for Governments to take the initiative \underline{themselves}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.118} You use a reflexive pronoun to emphasize that someone did something without any help or interference from anyone else.

In this use, the reflexive pronoun is normally placed at the end of the clause.
\begin{example}
    She had printed the card \underline{herself}.
    
    I'll take it down to the police station \underline{myself}.
    
    Did you make these \underline{yourself}?
\end{example}

\subsection{People in general: generic pronouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.119} When you want to talk about people in general, you can use a \textbf{generic pronoun}.

%
Here is a list of pronouns that can be used as generic pronouns:
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|l|l|}
        \hline
        \textbf{Subject} & you & one & we & they \\
        \hline
        \textbf{Object} & you & one & us & them \\
        \hline
        \textbf{Possessive} & yours & - & ours & theirs \\
        \hline
        \textbf{Reflexive} & yourself & oneself & ourselves & themselves \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.120} \textit{You} is sometimes used to make statements about people in general, or about a person in a situation that you are imaging.

\textit{You} is used in this way in both subject and object position.

The possessive form \textit{yours} and the reflexive form \textit{yourself} can also be used as generic pronouns.
\begin{example}
    To be a good doctor \underline{you} need to have good communication skills.
    
    Champagne can give \underline{you} a headache.
    
    Once \underline{you}'ve bought a physical book, \underline{you} own it: \underline{you} can lend it to people, donate it, and, well, it's \underline{yours}.
    
    When \underline{you} live alone \underline{you} have to force \underline{yourself} to go out more.
\end{example}
In informal English, you can use \textit{you get} or \textit{you have} to make a general statement about something that exists in the world.
\begin{example}
    \underline{You get} some old people who are very difficult.
    
    Anytime \underline{you have} over 8 inches of snow, driving becomes problematic.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.121} In formal writing, \textit{one} is sometimes used instead of \textit{you}.

\textit{Once} can be used in subject or object position.

The reflexive form \textit{oneself} is also used, but there is no possessive pronoun form of \textit{one}.
\begin{example}
    Going round Italy, \underline{one} is struck by the number of opera houses there are.
    
    This scene makes \underline{one} realize how deeply this community has been afflicted.
    
    If \underline{one} puts \underline{oneself} up for public office, then it is inappropriate that \underline{one} should behave badly.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.122} You can refer to a general group that includes yourself as \textit{we} or, in object position, \textit{us}.

This group can be all of mankind, or it can be smaller group e.g. a nation or a community.

The possessive form \textit{ours} and the reflexive form \textit{ourselves} can also be used in this way.
\begin{example}
    \underline{We} all need money.
    
    This survey gives \underline{us} insight into our attitudes and behavior as a nation.
    
    No other language has ever advanced as far, as fast, as \underline{ours}.
    
    \underline{We} need a change of government; just riding \underline{ourselves} of the prime minister isn't enough.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.123} You can use \textit{they} to refer to people in general.
\begin{example}
    Isn't that what \underline{they} call love?
\end{example}
\textit{They} can also refer to a group of people whose identity does not need to be stated.

E.g., in the sentence \textit{They've given John another pay rise} it is clear that \textit{they} refers to John's employers.
\begin{example}
    `Don't worry', I said to Mother, `\underline{they} are moving you from this ward soon.'
\end{example}
You can also use \textit{they} in subject position, or \textit{them} in object position, to refer to an individual when you do not know, or do not want to specify, whether the individual is male or female.

The possessive form \textit{theirs} and the reflexive form \textit{themselves} can also be used in this way.

This use is very common after:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the \textbf{indefinite pronouns} \textit{anyone, anybody, someone} and \textit{somebody}.
    
    These are explained in paragraphs \textbf{1.128--1.141}.
    \item singular nouns e.g. \textit{person, parent} and \textit{teacher}, which do not specify whether the person is male or female
    \begin{itemize}
        \item If \underline{anyone} wants to be a childminder, \underline{they} must attend a course.
        
        If I think \underline{someone} is having problems, I will spend hours talking to \underline{them}.
        
        \underline{A person}'s body fat determines how long \underline{they} can withstand cold water.
    \end{itemize}
\end{itemize}
Note that the plural form of the verb is always used after \textit{they}, even when it refers to only 1 person.

%
You can also use \textit{they, them, theirs} and \textit{themselves} to refer to:
\begin{itemize}
    \item the \textbf{indefinite pronouns} \textit{everyone, everybody, no one} and \textit{nobody}.
    
    These are explained in paragraphs \textbf{1.128--1.141}.
    \item noun phrases with the determiners \textit{each, every} and \textit{any}.
\end{itemize}
In these cases, although the pronouns or noun phrases are grammatically singular, they refer to groups of people.
\begin{example}
    I never avoid my obligations and I expect \underline{everyone} else to meet \underline{theirs}.
    
    \underline{Each parent} was sent an individual letter informing \underline{them} of the situation.
    
    We want \underline{every player} to push \underline{themselves} to get into the team.
\end{example}
Another way is to use \textit{he or she} instead of \textit{they, him or her} instead of \textit{them, himself or herself} instead of \textit{themselves} and \textit{his or hers} instead of \textit{theirs}.

This is often used in formal or written English.
\begin{example}
    Would a young person be able to get a job in Europe? That would depend on which country \underline{he or she} wanted to go to.
    
    The student should feel that the essay belongs to \underline{him or her}.
\end{example}
Some people use \textit{he} and \textit{him} in general statements or after indefinite pronouns, but many people object to this use because it suggests that person being referred to is male.

\subsection{Referring to a particular person or thing: \textit{this, that, these} and \textit{those}}
\noindent\fbox{1.125} When \textit{this, that, these}, and \textit{those} are used as pronouns, they are used as the subject or the object of a clause, or the object of a preposition.

%
Demonstrative pronouns are rarely used as the indirect object of a clause, because the indirect object is usually a person, and demonstrative pronouns normally refer to things.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.125} \textit{This} and \textit{that} are usually used as pronouns only when they refer to things.

You use them instead of a singular countable noun or an uncountable noun.

\textit{This} refers to something that is close to you in place or time, and \textit{that} refers to something that is more distant in place or time.
\begin{example}
    \underline{This} is a list of the rules.
    
    \underline{This} is the most important part of the job.
    
    The biggest problem was the accent. \underline{That} was difficult for me.
    
    \underline{That} looks interesting.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.126} \textit{This} and \textit{that} are used as pronouns to refer to a person when you are identifying someone or asking who they are.
\begin{example}
    Who's \underline{this}?
    
    He stopped and looked at a photograph that stood on the dressing table. Is \underline{this} your wife?
    
    Was \underline{that} Patrick on the phone?
\end{example}
When you are introducing people, you can say \textit{This is Mary} or \textit{This is Mr and Mrs Baker}.

Note that you use \textit{this} even when you are introducing more than 1 person.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.127} \textit{These} and \textit{those} can be used as pronouns instead of a plural countable noun.

They are most often used to refer to things, although they can be used to refer to people.

\textit{These} refers to a number of people and things that are close to you in place or time, and \textit{those} refers to a number of people and things that are more distant in place or time.
\begin{example}
    `I brought you \underline{these}.' Adam held out a bag of grapes.
    
    Vitamin tablets usually contain vitamins A, C, and D. \underline{These} are available from any child health clinic.
    
    \underline{These} are no ordinary students.
    
    It may be impossible for them to pay essential bills, e.g. \underline{those} for heating.
    
    \underline{Those} are easy questions to answer.
    
    There are a lot of people who are seeking employment, and a great number of \underline{those} are married women.
\end{example}
\textit{This, that, these}, and \textit{those} can also be \textbf{definite determiners}.

For more information, see paragraphs \textbf{1.184--1.193}.

See also \textit{Referring back} and \textit{Referring forward} in Chap. 10.

\subsection{Referring to people \& things in a non-specific way: \textit{someone, anyone, everyone}, etc.}
\noindent\fbox{1.128} When you want to refer to people or things but you do not know exactly who or what they are, or their identity is not important, you can use an \textbf{indefinite pronoun} e.g. \textit{someone, anyone}, or \textit{everyone}.

An indefinite pronoun shows only whether you are talking about people or about things, rather than referring to a specific person or thing.
\begin{example}
    I was there for over an hour before \underline{anybody} came.
    
    Jack was waiting for \underline{something}.
\end{example}
Here is a list of indefinite pronouns:
\begin{example}
    I was there for over an hour before \underline{anybody} came.
    
    Jack was waiting for \underline{something}.
\end{example}
Here is a list of indefinite pronouns:
\begin{example}
    anybody, anyone, anything, everybody, everyone, everything, nobody, no one, nothing, somebody, someone, something
\end{example}
\textbf{[AE]} Note that all indefinite pronouns are written as 1 word except \textit{no one}.

It is always 2 words in American English, but in British English it can also be spelled with a hyphen: \textit{no-one}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.129} You always use singular verbs with indefinite pronouns.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Is} anyone here?
    
    Everybody \underline{recognizes} the importance of education.
    
    Everything \underline{was} ready.
    
    Nothing \underline{is} certain in this world.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.130} You use the indefinite pronouns ending in \textit{-thing} to talk about objects, ideas, situations, or activities.
\begin{example}
    Can I do \underline{anything}?
    
    Jane said \underline{nothing} for a moment.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.131} You use the indefinite pronouns ending in \textit{-one} and \textit{-body} to refer to people.
\begin{example}
    It had to be \underline{someone} like Dan.
    
    Why does \underline{everybody} believe in the law of gravity?
\end{example}
Note that indefinite pronouns ending in \textit{-body} are more frequent in informal English.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.132} Although you use singular verbs with indefinite pronouns, if you want to refer back to an indefinite pronoun, you use the plural pronouns \textit{they, them}, or \textit{themselves}, or the possessive determiner \textit{their}.
\begin{example}
    Ask \underline{anyone}. \underline{They}'ll tell you.
    
    There's no way of telling \underline{somebody} why \underline{they}'ve failed.
    
    \underline{No one} liked being young then as \underline{they} do now.
    
    \underline{Everybody}'s enjoying \underline{themselves}.
    
    \underline{Everyone} put \underline{their} pens down.
\end{example}
See paragraph \textbf{1.123} for more information about \textit{they} used to refer to 1 person.

%
\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.133} In more formal English, some people prefer to use \textit{he, him, his}, or \textit{himself} to refer back to an indefinite pronoun, but many people dislike this use because it suggests that the person being referred to is male.
\begin{example}
    If \underline{someone} consistently eats a lot of fatty foods, it is not surprising if \underline{he} ends up with clogged arteries.
    
    \underline{Everybody} has \underline{his} dream.
\end{example}
For other ways of using pronouns when you do not want to specify whether the person you are talking about is male or female, see paragraph \textbf{1.123}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.134} You add \textit{'s} (apostrophe \textit{s}) to indefinite pronoun to refer to things that belong to or are associated with people.
\begin{example}
    She was given a room in \underline{someone's} studio.
    
    That was \underline{nobody's} business.
    
    I would defend \underline{anyone's} rights.
    
    Everything has been arranged to \underline{everybody's} satisfaction.
\end{example}
%
\textbf{[!] Be careful.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.135} You do not usually add \textit{'s} to indefinite pronouns referring to things.

So, e.g., you would be more likely to say \textit{the value of something} than \textit{something's value}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.136} When you want to give more information about the person or thing referred to by an indefinite pronoun, you can use a phrase or a clause after it.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Anyone over the age of 18} can apply.
    
    He would prefer to have \underline{somebody who had a background in the humanities}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.137} You can also use adjectives to add information.

Note that adjectives are placed after the indefinite pronoun rather than in front of it, and that you do not use a determiner.

You do not say \textit{an important someone}, you say \textit{someone important}.
\begin{example}
    What was needed was \underline{someone} practical.
    
    They are doing \underline{everything} possible to take care of you.
    
    There is \underline{nothing} wrong with being popular.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.138} If you have already mentioned a person or thing, and you want to refer to a different person or thing, or an additional one, you use \textit{else} after an indefinite pronoun.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Somebody else} will have to go out there.
    
    She couldn't think of \underline{anything else}.
    
    Everyone knows what \underline{everyone else} is doing.
    
    He got that job because \underline{nobody else} wanted it.
\end{example}
Note that if you want to show association or possession with an indefinite pronoun and \textit{else}, you add the \textit{'s} to \textit{else}.
\begin{example}
    Problems always became \underline{someone else's} fault.
    
    No one has control over \underline{anyone else's} career.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.139} Just like nouns, indefinite pronouns are the subject, object, or indirect object of clauses.

They can also be used as the objects of prepositions.

The indefinite pronouns beginning with \textit{some-} and \textit{every-} are most often used in affirmative clauses.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Everything} went according to plan.
    
    I remember \underline{somebody} putting a pillow under my head.
    
    `Now you'll see \underline{something},' he said.
    
    I gave \underline{everyone} a generous helping.
    
    I want to introduce you to \underline{someone} who is helping me.
    
    Is \underline{everything} all right?
\end{example}
They are sometimes used as the subject of a negative clause.
\begin{example}
    He could tell that \underline{something} wasn't right.
\end{example}
Note that the indefinite pronouns beginning with \textit{some-} cannot be used as the object of a negative clause, unless they are followed by a phrase or a clause.
\begin{example}
    He wasn't \underline{someone} I admired as a writer.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.140} Indefinite pronouns beginning with \textit{any-} can be used as the object or indirect object of a question or a negative clause.
\begin{example}
    Don't worry - I won't tell \underline{anyone}.
    
    You still haven't told me \underline{anything}.
    
    Take a good look and tell me if you see \underline{anything} different.
    
    I haven't given \underline{anyone} their presents yet.
\end{example}
They are often used as the subject of both negative and affirmative questions.

Note that they are not used as the subject of a negative statement.

You do not say, e.g., \textit{Anybody can't come in}.
\begin{example}
    Does \underline{anybody} agree with me?
    
    Won't \underline{anyone} help me?
    
    If \underline{anything} unusual happens, could you call me on this number?
\end{example}
Note that when you are making an affirmative statement, \textit{anyone} and \textit{anybody} are used to refer to people in general and not to only 1 person.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Anybody} who wants to can come in and buy a car from me.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.141} Indefinite pronouns beginning with \textit{no-} are always used with the affirmative form of a verb, and they make the clause negative.

For more information on \textbf{negative statements}, see paragraphs \textbf{5.49--5.93}.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Nobody} said a word.
    
    There was \underline{nothing} you could do, \underline{nothing} at all.
    
    She was to see \underline{no one}, to speak to \underline{nobody}, not even her own children.
\end{example}
Note that they are sometimes used in questions.

When this is the case, the answer to the question is usually expected to be \textit{no}.
\begin{example}
    `Is there \underline{nothing} I can do?' - `Not a thing'.
    
    `Is there \underline{nobody} else?' - `Not that I know of'.
\end{example}

\subsection{Showing that people do the same thing: \textit{each other} \& \textit{one another}}
\noindent\fbox{1.142} \textit{Each other} and \textit{one another} are called \textbf{reciprocal pronouns}.

They are used to say that people do the same thing, feel the same way, or have the same relationship.

%
E.g., if your brother hates your sister and your sister hates your brother, you can say \textit{My brother and sister hate each other} or \textit{They hate one another}.

%
Reciprocal pronouns are not used as the subject of a clause.

You can use them as the object or indirect object of a verb.
\begin{example}
    We help \underline{each other} a lot.
    
    You and I understand \underline{each other}.
    
    We support \underline{one another} through good times and bad.
    
    They sent \underline{each other} gifts from time to time.
\end{example}
You also use them as the object of a preposition.
\begin{example}
    Terry and Mark were jealous of \underline{each other}.
    
    The 2 lights were moving towards \underline{one another}.
    
    They didn't dare to look at \underline{one another}.
\end{example}
Some verbs are very commonly used with reciprocal pronouns.

For more information about these, see paragraphs \textbf{3.68--3.72}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.143} Note that there is very little difference between \textit{each other} and \textit{one another}.

They are both used to talk about 2 or more people or things, although some people prefer the use of \textit{each other} when there are only 2 people or things, and \textit{one another} when there are more than 2.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.144} In formal written English, you can also use \textit{each} as the subject of a clause and \textit{the other} as the object of a clause or preposition.

So, a more formal way of saying \textit{They looked at each other} is \textit{Each looked at the other}.

Note that \textit{each} is always followed by a singular verb.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Each} accuses \underline{the other} of lying.
    
    \underline{Each} is unwilling to learn from the experience of \underline{the others}.
\end{example}
\textit{Each} is also a determiner.

For more information about this, see paragraph \textbf{1.243}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.145} You add \textit{'s} (apostrophe \textit{s}) to \textit{each other, one another}, and \textit{the other} to form possessives.
\begin{example}
    I hope that you all enjoy \underline{each other's} company.
    
    Apes spend a great deal of time grooming \underline{one another's} fur.
    
    The males fight fiercely, each trying to seize \underline{the other's} long neck in its beak.
\end{example}

\subsection{Joining clauses together: relative pronouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.146} When a sentence consists of a main clause followed by a \textbf{relative clause} introduced by \textit{who, whom, which}, or \textit{that}, these words are called \textbf{relative pronouns}.

%
Relative pronouns do 2 things at the same time.

Like other pronouns, they refer to someone or something that has already been mentioned.

At the same time they join clauses together.

%
For more information about relative clauses, see paragraphs \textbf{8.83--8.116}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.147} \textit{Who} and \textit{whom} always refer to people.

%
\textit{Who} can be the subject of a relative clause.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ mathematicians \underline{who} are concerned with very difficult problems.
\end{example}
In the past, \textit{whom} was normally used as the object of a relative clause.

Nowadays, \textit{who} is more often used, although some careful speakers of English think that this is more correct to use \textit{whom}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a man \underline{who} I met recently.
    
    He's the man \underline{who} I saw last night.
    
    $\ldots$ 2 girls \underline{whom} I met in Edinburgh.
\end{example}
\textit{Who} is sometimes used as the object of a preposition when the object is separated from the preposition.

Some careful speakers think that it is more correct to use \textit{whom}.
\begin{example}
    That's the man \underline{who} I gave it to.
    
    $\ldots$ those \underline{whom} we cannot talk to.
\end{example}
\textit{Whom} is almost always used when the object comes immediately after the preposition.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ Lord Scarman, a man \underline{for whom} I have immense respect.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.148} \textit{Which} always refers to things.

It can be used as the subject or object of a relative clause, or as the object of a preposition.

\textit{Which} is often used in British English to introduce relative clauses that refer to things.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a region \underline{which} was threatened by growing poverty.
    
    $\ldots$ 2 horses \underline{which he owned}.
    
    $\ldots$ the house in \underline{which} I was born.
\end{example}
Note that \underline{which} cannot be used as the indirect object of a clause.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.149} \textit{That} refers to either people or things.

It is used as the subject or the object of a relative clause or the object of a preposition.

\textit{That} is generally preferred in American English to introduce relative clauses that refer to things or to combinations of people and things.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ the games \underline{that} politicians play.
    
    He's the boy \underline{that} sang the solo last night.
    
    It was the 1st bed \underline{that} she had ever slept in.
\end{example}
\textit{That} cannot be used as the indirect object of a clause.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.150} \textit{Whose} shows who or what something belongs to or is connected with.

Note that it cannot be used by itself, but must come in front of a noun.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ the thousands \underline{whose} lives have been damaged.
    
    There was a chap there \underline{whose} name I've forgotten.
    
    $\ldots$ predictions \underline{whose} accuracy will have to be determined.
    
    $\ldots$ sharks, \underline{whose} brains are minute.
\end{example}
Note that \textit{whose} is not restricted to people.

\subsection{Asking questions: interrogative pronouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.151} 1 way of asking questions i by using an \textbf{interrogative pronoun}.

%
The interrogative pronouns are \textit{who, whose, whom, what}, and \textit{which}.

They are used as the subject or object of a clause, or as the object of a preposition.

\textit{Whose} and \textit{which} are also determiners.

Other words, e.g. \textit{where, when, why}, and \textit{how}, are also used to ask questions.

%
Interrogative pronouns are not used as the indirect object of a clause.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Who} was at the door?
    
    `There's a cat outside.' - `\underline{Whose} is it?'
    
    \underline{Whom} do you support?
    
    \underline{Which} is best, gas or electric?
\end{example}
For more information about structures in which interrogative pronouns are used, see paragraphs \textbf{5.10--5.36}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.152} Interrogative pronouns refer to the information you are asking for.

%
\textit{Who, whose}, and \textit{whom} are used when you think that the answer to the question will be a person.
\begin{example}
    `He lost his wife.' - `\underline{Who}? Terry?'
    
    He looked at the cat. \underline{Whose} is it? Have you ever seen it before?
    
    `To \underline{whom}, if I may ask, are you engaged to be married?' - `To Danial Orton.'
\end{example}
\textit{Which} and \textit{what} are used when you think that the answer to the question will be something other than a person.
\begin{example}
    Is there really a difference? \underline{Which} do you prefer?
    
    `\underline{What} did he want?' - `Maurice's address.'
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.153} Interrogative pronouns are also used to introduce reported questions.
\begin{example}
    I asked her \underline{who} she had been talking to.
    
    He wondered \underline{what} Daintry would do now.
\end{example}
For more information about \textbf{reported questions}, see paragraphs \textbf{7.32--7.38}.

\subsection{Other pronouns}
\noindent\fbox{1.154} Many other words can be pronouns, provided that it is clear what is being talked about, because it is then unnecessary to repeat the noun.

%
E.g., most \textbf{indefinite determiners} can also be pronouns.

For more information about indefinite determiners, see paragraphs \textbf{1.223--1.250}.

%
Here is a list of indefinite determiners that are also pronouns:
\begin{example}
    all, another, any, both, each, either, enough, few, fewer, less, little, many, more, most, much, neither, several, some
\end{example}
Like all noun phrases, they can be used as the subject, direct object, or indirect object of a clause, or the object of a preposition.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Both} were offered jobs immediately.
    
    Children? I don't think she has \underline{any}.
    
    I saw 1 girl whispering to \underline{another}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.155} Although \textit{a, an, every}, and \textit{no} are indefinite determiners, they cannot stand alone as pronouns.

%
To refer back to a noun phrase that includes the determiner \textit{a} or \textit{an}, you can use the pronoun \textit{one}.

Similarly, you use \textit{each} to refer back to a noun phrase that includes \textit{every}, and \textit{none} to refer back to a noun phrase that includes \textit{no}.

%
Note that \textit{another} and \textit{others} are pronouns, but \textit{other} cannot be a pronoun.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.156} \textit{All, both}, and \textit{each} can be used in addition to nouns or personal pronouns for emphasis, in a similar way to use of \textbf{reflexive pronouns} described in paragraphs \textbf{1.116--1.118}.
\begin{example}
    The brothers \underline{all} agreed that something more was needed.
    
    He loved them \underline{both}.
    
    Ford and Duncan \underline{each} had their chances.
\end{example}
They come after an auxiliary verb, a model, or \textit{be}.
\begin{example}
    They \underline{were both} still working at their universities.
    
    The letters \underline{have all} been signed.
    
    The older children \underline{can all} do the same things together.
\end{example}
\textit{Each} can also come at the end of the clause.
\begin{example}
    3 others were fined \pounds200 \underline{each}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.157} \textbf{Numbers} can also be pronouns.

E.g., the answer to the question \textit{How many children do you have?} is usually \textit{3} rather than \textit{3 children}.
\begin{example}
    `How many people are there?' - `\underline{45}.'
    
    Of the other women, \underline{2} are dancers.
    
    They bought 8 companies and sold of \underline{5}.
\end{example}
For more information on numbers, see paragraphs \textbf{2.213--2.231}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.158} The number \textit{one} is a special case.

Like other numbers, it is sometimes used to refer to 1 of a group of things.

%
\textit{One} is also used to refer back to a noun phrase with the determiner \textit{a}.
\begin{example}
    Could I have a bigger \underline{one}, please?
\end{example}
It can also be used for emphasis after another determiner.
\begin{example}
    There are systems of communication right through the animal world; \underline{each one} is distinctive.
\end{example}
\textit{One} can be used as a \textbf{personal pronoun}.

This use is explained in paragraph \textbf{1.121}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.159} Note that \textit{the one} and \textit{the ones} can be used to refer to a noun alone, rather than to the whole of the noun phrase.

They are nearly always used with a modifier e.g. an adjective, or some form of information after the noun, e.g. a prepositional phrase.
\begin{example}
    `Which poem?' - `\underline{The one} they were talking about yesterday.'
    
    There are 3 bedrooms. Mine is \underline{the one} at the back.
    
    He gave the best seats to \underline{the ones} who arrived 1st.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.160} You use \textit{the other, the others, others}, or \textit{another} to refer to different members of a group of things or people.
\begin{example}
    Some writers are greater than \underline{others}.
    
    1 runner was way ahead of all \underline{the others}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.161} If you want to say something about a member of a group of people or things you can use \textit{one}.

You can then refer to the rest of the group as \textit{the others}.
\begin{example}
    The bells are carefully installed so that disconnecting one will have no effect on \underline{the others}.
    
    They had 3 little daughters, \underline{one} a baby, \underline{the others} twins of 12.
\end{example}
You use \textit{the one} and \textit{the other} to refer to each of a pair of things.
\begin{example}
    The same factors push wages and prices up together, \underline{the one} reinforcing \underline{the other}.
\end{example}
If you do not wish to specify exactly which of a group you are talking about, you refer to \textit{one or other} of them.
\begin{example}
    It may be that \underline{one or other} of them had fears for their health.
\end{example}

\section{Definite \& indefinite determiners}
\noindent\fbox{1.162} In English, there are 2 main ways in which you can use a \textbf{noun phrase}.

You can use it to refer to someone or something, knowing that the person you are speaking to understands which person or thing you are talking about.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The man} began to run towards \underline{the boy}.
    
    Young people don't like \underline{these operas}.
    
    Thank you very much for \underline{your comments}.
    
    $\ldots$ a visit to \underline{the Houses of Parliament}.
\end{example}
Alternatively, you can use a noun phrase to refer to someone or something of a particular type, without saying which person or thing you mean.
\begin{example}
    There was \underline{a man} in the lift.
    
    I wish I'd bought \underline{an umbrella}.
    
    \underline{Any doctor} would say she didn't know what she was doing.
\end{example}
In order to distinguish between these 2 ways of using a noun phrase, you use a special class of words called \textbf{determiners}.

There are 2 types of determiner, \textbf{definite determiners} and \textbf{indefinite determiners}.

You put them at the beginning of a noun phrase.

\subsection{Using the definite determiner \textit{the}}
\noindent\fbox{1.163} \textit{The} is the most common definite determiner; it is sometimes called the \textbf{definite article}.

%
\textit{This, that, these}, and \textit{those} are often called \textbf{demonstratives} or \textbf{demonstrative adjectives}.

For more information on these, see paragraphs \textbf{1.184--1.193}.

%
\textit{My, your, his, her, its, our} and \textit{their} are \textbf{possessive determiners}.

They are also sometimes called \textbf{possessive adjectives} or just \textbf{possessives}.

For more information about these, see paragraphs \textbf{1.194--1.210}.

%
Here is the list of definite determiners:
\begin{example}
    the
    
    this, that, these, those
    
    my, your, his, her, its, our, their
\end{example}
Note that in English you cannot use more than 1 definite determiner before a noun.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.164} Because \textit{the} is the most common definite determiner, you can put \textit{the} in front of any common noun.
\begin{example}
    She dropped \underline{the can} into \underline{the grass}.
    
    \underline{The girls} were not in \underline{the house}.
\end{example}
In these examples, the use of \textit{the can} means that a can has already been mentioned; \textit{the grass} is definite because it has already been stated that \textit{she} is outside, and the presence of grass may also have been stated or is presumed; \textit{the girls}, like \textit{the can}, must have been mentioned before; and \textit{the house} means the one where the girls were staying at the time.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.165} \textit{The} always has the same spelling, but it has 3 different pronunciations:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textipa{/D@/} when the following word begins with a consonant sound
    \begin{example}
        $\ldots$ the dictionary$\ldots$ the 1st act$\ldots$ the big box
    \end{example}
    \item \textipa{/Di/} when the following word begins with a vowel sound
    \begin{example}
        $\ldots$ the exhibition$\ldots$ the effect$\ldots$ the impression
    \end{example}
    \item \textipa{/Di:/} when it is emphasized
    \begin{example}
        You don't mean \underline{the} Ernest Hemingway?
    \end{example}
\end{itemize}
See paragraph \textbf{1.181} for more information about emphatic uses of \textit{the}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.166} You can use a noun phrase consisting just of \textit{the} and a noun when you know that the person you are talking or writing to will understand which person, thing, or group you are referring to.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The expedition} sailed out into \underline{the Pacific}.
    
    $\ldots$ the most obnoxious boy in \underline{the school}.
    
    He stopped \underline{the car} in front of \underline{the bakery}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.167} Some nouns are used with \textit{the} because they refer to only 1 person, thing, or group.

Some of these are specific names or \textbf{proper nouns}, e.g. titles e.g. \textit{the Pope}, unique things e.g. \textit{the Eiffel Tower}, and place names e.g. \textit{the Atlantic}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a concert attended by \underline{the Queen}.
    
    We went on camel rides to \underline{the Pyramids}.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{1.52--1.58} for more information about proper nouns.

%
Some are \textbf{singular nouns}, that is they refer to something of which there is only 1 in the world, e.g. \textit{the ground}, or \textit{the moon}.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The sun} began to turn red.
    
    In Apr and May \underline{the wind} blows steadily.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{1.34--1.40} for more information about singular nouns.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.168} Other nouns are used to talk about just 1 person, thing, or group in a particular place or organization, so that if you are talking about that place or organization or talking to someone in it, you can use just \textit{the} and the noun.

%
E.g., if there is only 1 station in a town, the people who live in the town will talk about \textit{the station}.

Similarly, people living in Britain talk to each other about \textit{the economy}, meaning \textit{the British economy}, and people working for the same organization might talk about \textit{the boss, the union}, or \textit{the canteen} without needing to specify the organization.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The church} has been broken into.
    
    There's a wind coming off \underline{the river}.
    
    We had to get rid of \underline{the director}.
    
    \underline{The mayor} is a 48-year-old former laborer.
    
    What is \underline{the President} doing about all this?
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.169} Normally, if you want to talk generally about all people or things of a particular type, you use the plural form of a noun without a determiner.

%
However, there are some countable nouns that are used in the singular with \textit{the} to refer to something more general.

%
E.g., you can use \textit{the theater} or \textit{the stage} to talk about all entertainment performed in theaters.

Similarly, \textit{the screen} refers to films in general, and \textit{the law} refers to the system of laws in a country.
\begin{example}
    For him, \underline{the stage} was just a way of earning a living.
    
    He was as handsome in real life as he was on \underline{the screen}.
    
    They do not hesitate to break \underline{the law}.
\end{example}
%
Some nouns that normally refer to an individual thing or person can be used in the singular with \textit{the} to refer generally to a system or service in a particular place.

E.g., you can use \textit{the bus} to talk about a bus service and \textit{the phone} to talk about a telephone system.
\begin{example}
    How long does it take on \underline{the train}?
    
    We rang for \underline{the ambulance}.
\end{example}
Nouns referring to musical instruments can be used in the singular with \textit{the} when you are talking about someone playing, or being able to play, a particular kind of instrument.
\begin{example}
    You play \underline{the oboe}, I see, said Simon.
    
    I was playing \underline{the piano} when he phoned.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.170} When you want to talk generally about groups of people who share the same characteristic or quality, you often choose an adjective preceded by \textit{the}.
\begin{example}
    This project is all about giving employment to \underline{the unemployed}.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{.166--1.72} for more information about using adjectives as nouns.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.171} Nouns referring to plants and animals can be used in the singular with \textit{the} when you are making a statement about every member of a species.

E.g., if you say \textit{The swift has long, narrow wings}, you mean that all swifts have long, narrow wings.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The primrose} can grow abundantly on chalk banks.
    
    Australia is the home of \underline{the kangaroo}.
\end{example}
Similarly, a noun referring to a part of the human body can be used with \textit{the} to refer to that part of anyone's body.
\begin{example}
    These arteries supply \underline{the heart} with blood.
    
    $\ldots$ the arteries supplying \underline{the kidneys}.
\end{example}
\textit{The} is sometimes used with other nouns in the singular to make a statement about all the members of a group.
\begin{example}
    The article focuses on how to protect \underline{the therapist} rather than on how to cure \underline{the patient}.
\end{example}
These uses are fairly formal.

They are not common in ordinary speech.

Usually, if you want to make a statement about all the things of a particular kind, you use the plural form of a noun without a determiner.

See paragraph \textbf{1.227} for more information about this.

\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.172} Many common time expressions consist of just \textit{the} and a noun.
\begin{example}
    We wasted a lot of money in \underline{the past}.
    
    The train leaves Cardiff at 4 in \underline{the afternoon}.
    
    $\ldots$ the changes which are taking place at \underline{the moment}.
\end{example}
See Chap. 4 for more information about time expressions.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.173} In each of the paragraphs above showing uses of \textit{the} and a noun, it is possible to understand who or what is being referred to because the noun phrase is commonly accepted as referring to 1 particular person, thing, or group.

%
However, you can use \textit{the} with any noun, if it is obvious who or what you are referring to from what has already been said or written.

%
The usual way of referring back to a noun is to use a pronoun, but if the 2nd reference does not come immediately after the 1st one, or if it is not immediately obvious which noun is being referred to, you should use \textit{the}, and the noun again.

E.g., if you have already mentioned that you were in a train, and then continued to tell a story, you can say afterwards \textit{The train suddenly stopped}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.174} You can also use \textit{the} and a noun when you are referring to someone or something closely connected with something you have just mentioned.

%
E.g., you do not usually say \textit{We tried to get into the room, but the door of the room was locked}.

You say \textit{We tried to get into the room, but the door was locked}, because it is obvious which door you are referring to.
\begin{example}
    She stopped and lit a match. The wind almost blew out \underline{the flame}.    
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.175} Although there are many situations where you use just \textit{the} and a noun, there are other occasions when you need to add something else to the noun in order to make it clear which person, thing, or group you are referring to.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.176} Sometimes you can show who or what you are referring to by putting an \textbf{adjective} between \textit{the} and the noun.
\begin{example}
    This is \underline{the main bedroom}.
    
    Somebody ought to have done it long ago, remarked \underline{the fat man}.
\end{example}
Sometimes you need to use more than 1 adjective.
\begin{example}
    After the crossroads look out for \underline{the large white building}.
\end{example}
For more information about adjectives, see paragraphs \textbf{2.2--2.168}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.177} When you use an adjective between \textit{the} and a noun, you do not always do it in order to make clear who or what is being referred to.

%
E.g., you might want to add further information about someone or something that you have already mentioned.

So, if you 1st refer to someone as \textit{a woman} in a sentence e.g. \textit{A woman came into the room}, you might later want to refer to her as \textit{the unfortunate woman} or \textit{the smiling woman}.

%
This is a very common use in written English, especially in stories, but it is not often used in conversation.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The astonished waiter} was now watching from the other end of the room.
    
    \underline{The poor woman} had witnessed terrible violence.
    
    The loss of pressure caused \underline{the speeding car} to go into a skid.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.178} Another way of showing who or what you are referring to is buy adding extra information after \textit{the} and a noun, e.g. a prepositional phrase, a relative clause, a \textit{to}-infinitive, an adverb of place or time, or a phrase introduced by a participle.

%
So you might refer to particular people at a party by using noun phrases e.g. \textit{the girl in the yellow dress, the woman who spilled her drink}, or \textit{the man smoking a cigar}.
\begin{example}
    \underline{The cars in the driveways} were all Ferraris and Porsches.
    
    \underline{The book that I recommend} now costs over 20 pounds.
    
    \underline{The thing to aim for} is an office of your own.
    
    Who made the bed in \underline{the room upstairs}?
    
    It depends on \underline{the person being interviewed}.
\end{example}
For more information about this type of extra information that is added to a noun, see paragraphs \textbf{2.272--2.302}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.179} You do not normally use \textit{the} with \textbf{uncountable nouns} because they refer to something in a general way.

However, \textit{the} is necessary if the uncountable noun is followed by extra information e.g. clause or a phrase that relates it to a particular person, thing, or group.

%
E.g., you cannot say \textit{I am interested in education of young children},

You have to say \textit{I am interested in the education of young children}.
\begin{example}
    Babies need \underline{the comfort of their mother's arms}.
    
    Even \underline{the honesty of Inspector Butler} was in doubt.
    
    I've no idea about \underline{the geography of Scotland}.
\end{example}
For more information about uncountable nouns, see paragraphs \textbf{1.23--1.33}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.180} \textit{The} is also used with \textbf{superlative adjectives}.
\begin{example}
    I'm not \underline{the best cook in the world}.
    
    They went to \underline{the most expensive restaurant in town}.
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{2.112--2.122} for more information about superlative adjectives.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.181} \textit{The} is often used in front of a noun to indicate that someone or something is the best of its kind.
\begin{example}
    New Zealand is now \underline{the place to visit}.
\end{example}
You can also use \textit{the} in front of a person's name to show that you are referring to the most famous person with that name.
\begin{example}
    You actually met \underline{the George Harrison}?
\end{example}
When you use \textit{the} in either of these ways, you emphasize it and pronounce it \textipa{/Di:/}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.182} \textit{The} can be used in front of some \textbf{indefinite determiners}, usually to give an indication of amount or quantity.

%
These indefinite determiners are:
\begin{example}
    few, little, many, other
\end{example}

\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ pleasures known only to \underline{the few}.
    
    $\ldots$ a coup under the leadership of \underline{the select few}.
    
    He was 1 of \underline{the few who knew where to find me}.
    
    We have done \underline{the little that is in our power}.
\end{example}
For more information about indefinite determiners, see paragraphs \textbf{1.223--1.250}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.183} \textit{The} is used with \textit{one} and \textit{ones}.
\begin{example}
    I'm going to have \underline{the green one}.
    
    The shop was different from \underline{the ones I remembered}.
    
    $\ldots$ a pair of those old glasses, \underline{the ones with those funny} square lenses.
\end{example}
\textit{The} is also used with other \textbf{numbers}.
\begin{example}
    It is a mistake to confuse \underline{the 2}.
    
    Why is she so different from \underline{the other 2}?
\end{example}
See paragraphs \textbf{2.208--2.239} for more information about numbers.

\subsection{Definite determiners: using \textit{this, that, these}, and \textit{those}}
\noindent\fbox{1.184} You use the \textbf{definite determiners} \textit{this, that, these}, and \textit{those} to talk about people or things in a definite way.

%
You use \textit{this} and \textit{these} to talk about people and things that are close to you in place or time.

When you talk about people or things that are more distant in place or time, you use \textit{that} and \textit{those}.

%
You put \textit{this} and \textit{that} in front of singular nouns, uncountable nouns, and the singular pronoun one.

You put \textit{these} and \textit{those} in front of plural nouns and the plural pronoun \textit{ones}.

%
\textit{This, that, these}, and \textit{those} are often called \textbf{demonstratives} or \textbf{demonstrative adjectives}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.185} \textit{This} and \textit{these} are used to talk about people or things that are very obvious in the situation that you are in.

E.g., if you are inside a house, you can refer to it as \textit{this house}.

If you are holding some keys in your hand, you can refer to them as \textit{these keys}.

If you are at a party, you can refer to it as \textit{this party}.
\begin{example}
    I have lived in \underline{this house} my entire life.
    
    I am going to walk up \underline{these steps} towards you.
    
    I'll come as soon as \underline{these men} have finished their work.
    
    I like \underline{this university}.
    
    Good evening. In \underline{this programme} we are going to look at the way in which British music has developed in recent years.
\end{example}
When it is clear who or what you are referring to, you can use \textit{this} and \textit{these} as \textbf{pronouns}.

This use is explained in paragraphs \textbf{1.124--1.127}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.186} \textit{This} and \textit{these} are also used in many expressions that refer to current periods of time, e.g. \textit{this month, this week}, and \textit{these days}.

This use is explained in Chap. 4.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.187} You use \textit{that} and \textit{those} when you are talking about things or people that you can see but that are not close to you.
\begin{example}
    How much is it for \underline{that big box}?
    
    Can I have one of \underline{those brochures}?
    
    Can you move \underline{those books} off there?
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.188} When it is clear who or what you are talking about, you can use \textit{that} and \textit{those} as \textbf{pronouns}.

This use is explained in paragraphs \textbf{1.124--1.127}.
\begin{example}
    Could you just hold \underline{that}?
    
    Please don't take \underline{those}.
\end{example}
%
\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.189} You can show that you are referring to the same person or thing you have just mentioned by using \textit{this, that, these}, or \textit{those} in front of a noun.

E.g., if you have just mentioned a girl, you can refer to her as \textit{this girl} or \textit{that girl} the 2nd time you mention here.

Normally, you use a \textbf{pronoun} to refer to someone or something you have just mentioned, but sometimes you cannot do this because it might not be clear who or what the pronoun refers to.
\begin{example}
    Students and staff suggest books for the library, and normally we're quite happy to get \underline{those books}.
    
    Their house is in a valley. The people in \underline{that valley} speak about the people in the next valley as foreigners.
    
    They had a lot of diamonds, and they asked her if she could possibly get \underline{these diamonds} to Britain.
\end{example}
The use of \textit{this, that, those}, etc. to refer again to something that has already been mentioned is fully explained in paragraphs \textbf{10.7--10.10}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.190} In informal English, you can also use \textit{that} and \textit{those} in front of a noun to talk about people or things that are already known to the person you are speaking or writing to.
\begin{example}
    \underline{That idiot Antonio} has gone and locked our cabin door.
    
    Have they found \underline{those missing children} yet?
    
    Do you remember \underline{that funny little apartment}?
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.191} You can use \textit{that} in front of a noun when you are talking about something that has just happened.
\begin{example}
    I knew \underline{that meeting} would be difficult.
\end{example}
\textit{That} is used as a \textbf{pronoun} to talk about something that has just happened.

This use is explained in paragraphs \textbf{1.124--1.127}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.192} In more formal English, \textit{those} can be used instead of \textit{the} in front of a plural noun when the plural noun is followed by a \textbf{relative clause}.

In this use, the relative clause specifies exactly which group of people or things are being referred to.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ \underline{those workers who are employed in large enterprises}.
    
    The parents are not afraid to be firm about \underline{those matters that seem} important to them.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.193} In informal spoken English, people sometimes use \textit{this} and \textit{these} in front of nouns even when they are mentioning someone or something for the 1st time.
\begin{example}
    And then \underline{this woman} came up to me and she said, I believe you have a goddaughter called Celia Ravenscroft.
    
    At school we had to wear \underline{these awful white cotton hats}.
\end{example}

\subsection{Possessive determiners: \textit{my, your, their}, etc.}
\noindent\fbox{1.194} You often want to show that a thing belongs to someone or that it is connected in some way with someone.

%
1 way of doing this is to use a word like \textit{my, your}, and \textit{their}, which tells you who something belongs to.

These words are called \textbf{possessive determiners}.
\begin{example}
    Are \underline{your children} bilingual?
    
    I remember \underline{his name} now.
    
    They would be welcome to use \underline{our library}.
    
    I'd been waiting a long time to park \underline{my car}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.195} There are 7 possessive determiners in English, and each one is associated with a particular personal pronoun:
\begin{table}[H]
    \centering
    \begin{tabular}{|l|l|l|}
        \hline
        & \textbf{singular} & \textbf{plural} \\
        \hline
        \textbf{1st person} & my & our \\
        \hline
        \textbf{2nd person} & your & your \\
        \hline
        \textbf{3rd person} & his, her, its & their \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}
Personal pronouns are explained in paragraphs \textbf{1.95--1.106}.

%
\textbf{[!] Be careful.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.196} You do not spell the possessive \textit{its} with an apostrophe.

\textit{It's} is short for \textit{it is}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.197} Possessive determiners, like other determiners, come after any words like \textit{all} or \textit{some of} (called \textbf{predeterminers}), and before any numbers or adjectives.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ all \underline{his} letters.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{their} next message.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{my} little finger.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{our} 2 lifeboats.
\end{example}
See paragraph \textbf{1.251} for more information about predeterminers.

%
\textbf{[!] Be careful.}

\noindent\fbox{1.200} When you want to draw attention to the fact that something belongs or relates to a particular person or thing, you can use the word \textit{own} after the possessive determiner.
\begin{example}
    I helped him to some more water but left \underline{my own glass} untouched.
    
    Residents are allowed to bring \underline{their own furniture} with them if they wish to do so.
    
    Make \underline{your own decisions}.
    
    I heard it with \underline{my own ears}.
    
    She felt in charge of \underline{her own affairs}.
\end{example}
If you use a number or adjective in this structure, you put the number or adjective after \textit{own}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ their \underline{own 3 children}.
    
    The players provided their \underline{own white shorts}.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.201} Possessive determiners do not always show that what follows them is actually possessed (or owned) by someone.

Sometimes they just show that what follows is connected or associated with someone in some way.
\begin{example}
    They then turned \underline{their attention} to other things.
    
    $\ldots$ the vitality of \underline{our music} and \underline{our culture}.
    
    In summer, hay fever interfaced with all \underline{her activities}.
    
    It's \underline{his brother} who has the workshop.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.202} You can use a possessive determiner in front of a noun that refers to an action, in order to show who or what is doing the action.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ not long after \underline{our arrival}.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{his criticism} of the Government.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{their fight} for survival.
    
    I'm waiting for \underline{your explanation}.
    
    Most of \underline{their claims} were worthy.
\end{example}
In the last example, \textit{their claims} refers to the claims that they have made.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.203} You can also use a possessive determiner to say who or what is affected by an action.
\begin{example}
    \underline{My appointment} as the 1st woman chairman symbolizes change.
    
    $\ldots$ the redistribution of wealth, rather than \underline{its creation}.
    
    They expressed their horror at \underline{her dismissal}.
\end{example}
In the last example, \textit{her dismissal} refers to the fact that she was dismissed by someone or by a company.

%
In the 1st of the following examples, \textit{his supporters} means the people who support him.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ Birch and \underline{his supporters}.
    
    She returned the ring to \underline{its owner}.
\end{example}
%
\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.204} Sometimes in English the determiner \textit{the} is used where there is an obvious possessive meaning.

In these cases the possession is already made clear by a preceding noun or pronoun.

The following paragraphs explain the situations in which you use \textit{the} rather than a possessive determiner.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.205} When you refer to a specific part of somebody's body, you normally use a possessive determiner.
\begin{example}
    She has something on \underline{her feet} and a bag in \underline{her hand}.
    
    Nancy suddenly took \underline{my arm}.
    
    The children wore nothing on \underline{their feet}.
    
    She thanked him shyly and patted \underline{his arm}.
    
    I opened the cupboard and they fell on \underline{my head}.
    
    He shook \underline{his head}.
\end{example}
However, when you are describing an action that someone does a part of someone else's body, you often use the definite article (\textit{the}), especially when the body part is the object of a preposition and when the object of the verb is a pronoun.

E.g., if you say \textit{She hit me on the head, head} is the object of the preposition \textit{on} and \textit{me} is the object of the verb \textit{hit}.
\begin{example}
    \fbox{I patted him on the head}.
    
    He \underline{took her by the arm} and began drawing her away.
\end{example}
You use the definite article because the \textit{owner} of the body part has already been identified, and you do not need to repeat this information.

%
Similarly, if the object of the verb is a \textbf{reflexive pronoun} e.g. \textit{myself, yourself}, and so on, you use the definite article.

This is because the reflexive pronoun already refers to you or to the person who is doing the action, so you do not need to repeat this information by using a possessive determiner.
\begin{example}
    I accidentally \underline{hit myself on the head} with the brush handle.
    
    We can \underline{pat ourselves on the back} for bringing up our children.
\end{example}
Uses of reflexive pronouns are explained in paragraphs \textbf{1.111--1.118}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.206} If you want to describe something that you do to yourself or that someone else does to themselves, you normally use a possessive determiner.
\begin{example}
    She was brushing \underline{her hair}.
    
    `I'm going to \underline{brush my teeth},' he said.
    
    She \underline{gritted her teeth} and carried on.
    
    He walked into the kitchen and \underline{shook his head}.
\end{example}
%
\textbf{[!] Be careful.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.207} You usually use possessive determiners when you refer to people or animals.

You do not usually use them to refer to things that are not alive.

It is, e.g., more usual to say \textit{the door} or \textit{the door of the room} than \textit{its door}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.208} Possessive determiners are sometimes used to talk about things that belong to or are associated with people in general, in a similar way to generic pronouns.

For more information on \textbf{generic pronouns}, see paragraphs \textbf{1.119--1.123}.

%
\textit{Your} can be used when you are talking about something that belongs to or is associated with people in general, or with a person in a situation that you are imagining.
\begin{example}
    Can eating a low-fat diet weaken \underline{your} hair?
    
    Going to the gym is good for \underline{your} general health.
    
    Part of the process involves discussing \underline{your} decision with a career counselor.
\end{example}
In more formal English, \textit{one's} is sometimes used instead of \textit{your}.
\begin{example}
    A satisfying job can bring structure and meaning to \underline{one's} life.
\end{example}
\textit{Our} can be used to talk about all of mankind or society.
\begin{example}
    Being a child is not easy in \underline{our} society.
\end{example}
\textit{Their} can be used to refer to a person when you do not know, or do not want to specify, whether that person is male or female.
\begin{example}
    The most important asset a person has is \underline{their} ability to work.
    
    Each winner received a plaque with \underline{their} award title.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.209} There are other ways of showing that something is owned by or connected with someone or something else.

E.g., you can use \textit{'s} (apostrophe \textit{s}) or a prepositional phrase beginning with \textit{of}.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Mary's daughter} is called Elizabeth.
    
    Very often the person appointed has \underline{no knowledge of that company's end product}.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{the house of a rich banker} in Paris.
    
    In \underline{the opinion of the team}, what would they consider to be absolutely necessary?
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.210} Possessive determiners are also sometimes used in titles e.g. \textit{Your Majesty} and \textit{His Excellency}.

This use is explained in paragraph \textbf{1.56}.

\subsection{The possessive form: apostrophe \textit{s} (\textit{'s})}
\noindent\fbox{1.211} You usually show that something belongs to or is associated with a specific, named person by adding \textit{'s} to the name of the person, and by placing this possessive form in front of the thing that belongs to them, or is associated with them.

E.g., if John owns a motorbike, you can refer to it is `John's motorbike'.
\begin{example}
    Sylvia put her hand on \underline{John's} arm.
    
    $\ldots$ the main features of \underline{Mr Brown's} economic policy.
\end{example}
When you use a noun rather than a name to refer to the person, the noun phrase containing the possessive form also contains a determiner in the usual way.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ \underline{his grandmother's} house.
    
    \underline{Your mother's} best handbag.
\end{example}
Note that the determiner applies to the possessive form and not to the noun that is being modified by the possessive.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.212} The spelling and pronunciation patterns used to form possessives change according to the spelling and pronunciation of the name or noun.

These are explained in the Reference section.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.213} Note that in addition ot being the possessive form, the pattern \textit{'s} can also be added to words as a contraction of \textit{is} or \textit{has}.

This is explained in the Reference section.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.214} Apostrophe \textit{s} (\textit{'s}) is most often added to a noun referring to a person or an animal.
\begin{example}
    I wore a pair of my \underline{sister's} boots.
    
    Philip watched his \underline{friend's} reaction.
    
    Billy patted the \underline{dog's} head.
\end{example}
It can also be used to show that something belongs to or is associated with a group of people or an institution.
\begin{example}
    She runs the foreign exchange desk for the \underline{banks} corporate clients.
    
    They also prepare the \underline{university's} budget.
    
    $\ldots$ the \underline{paper's} political editor, Mr Fred Emery.
    
    There was a raid on the \underline{Democratic Party's} headquarters.
    
    What is your \underline{government's} policy?
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.215} Apostrophe \textit{s} (\textit{'s}) is sometimes added to a noun referring to an object when specifying a part of it or a quality or feature that it has.
\begin{example}
    I like the \underline{car's} design.
    
    You can predict a \underline{computer's} behavior because it follows rules.
\end{example}
Apostrophe \textit{s} (\textit{'s}) is used after nouns and names referring to places to specify something in that place.
\begin{example}
    He is the administrative head of the \underline{country's} biggest city.
    
    The \underline{city's} population is in decline.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{Britain's} most famous company.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.216} If you want to emphasize that something belongs or relates to a particular person and nobody else, you use \textit{own}.

\textit{Own} can be used after the possessive form of a name or noun, as well as after a possessive determiner.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Prof. Wilson's own} answer may be unacceptable.
    
    We must depend on \underline{David's own} assessment.
\end{example}
If you are specifying a number of things, you put the number after \textit{own}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ the Doctor's \underline{own 2} rooms.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.217} When you are talking about 2 things of the same type that belong to different people you can use the possessive form of a name or noun like a possessive pronoun so that you can avoid repeating the thing itself.

In the 1st example below, \textit{her brother's} is used instead of \textit{her brother's appearance}.
\begin{example}
    Her appearance is very different to \underline{her brother's}.
    
    My room is next to \underline{Karen's}.
    
    It is your responsibility rather than \underline{your parents'}.
\end{example}
The possessive form can also be used on its own to refer to someone's home or place of work.
\begin{example}
    He's round at \underline{David's}.
    
    She stopped off at the \underline{butcher's} for a piece of steak.
    
    She hasn't been back to the \underline{doctor's} since.
\end{example}
\textbf{Possessive pronouns} are explained in paragraphs \textbf{1.107--1.110}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.218} The possessive form can be used in a prepositional phrase beginning with \textit{of} after a noun phrase.

You use this structure when you are talking about 1 of a number of things that belong to someone or are associated with them, rather than about something unique.
\begin{example}
    Julia, a friend \underline{of Jenny's}, was there too.
    
    That word has a favorite of \underline{of your father's}.
\end{example}
%
\textbf{[i] Usage note.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.219} Possessive forms can also be used to refer to things of a particular type that are usually associated with someone.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ a woman dressed in a \underline{man's} raincoat.
    
    $\ldots$ a \underline{policeman's} uniform.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{women's} magazines.
    
    $\ldots$ the \underline{men's} lavatory.
\end{example}
%
\noindent\fbox{1.220} The possessive form can sometimes be used with nouns that refer to an action in order to show who or what is performing the action.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ the \underline{banking service's} rapid growth.
    
    $\ldots$ \underline{Madeleine's} arrival at Fairwater House School.
\end{example}
Note that phrases beginning with \textit{of} are used more commonly to do this, and that they are more formal than this use of possessive forms.

The use of prepositional phrases beginning with \textit{of} to show who or what is performing an action is explained in paragraph \textbf{2.282}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.221} Sometimes you can add apostrophe \textit{s} (\textit{'s}) to a noun referring to the thing affected by the performer of an action and put it in front of the noun referring to that performer.

E.g., you can talk about \textit{the scheme's supporters}.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ \underline{Christ's} followers.
    
    $\ldots$ the \underline{car's} owner.
\end{example}
Sometimes an apostrophe \textit{s} (\textit{'s}) structure can be used to refer to the thing affected by an action.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ \underline{Capello's} appointment as England manager.
\end{example}
Note again that \textit{of} structures are more commonly used to do this.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.222} It is also possible to show possession by using either a prepositional phrase beginning with \textit{of}, or a structure with a noun modifier in it.

%
Prepositional phrases beginning with \textit{of} are explained in paragraphs \textbf{2.277--2.283}.

Noun modifiers are explained in paragraphs \textbf{2.169--2.174}.

\subsection{Indefinite determiners: \textit{all, some, many}, etc.}
\noindent\fbox{1.223} \textbf{Indefinite determiners} are used in noun phrases when you are talking about people or things in a general way, without identifying them.

%
Here is a list of indefinite determiners:
\begin{example}
    a, all, an, another, any, both, each, either, enough, every, few, little, many, more, most, much, neither, no, other, several, some
\end{example}
\textit{A}, and \textit{an} are the most common indefinite determiners; they are sometimes called the \textbf{definite article}.

For more information about \textit{a} and \textit{an}, see paragraphs \textbf{1.228--1.235}.

%
For more information about the other indefinite determiners, see paragraphs \textbf{1.236--1.250}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.224} \textit{A} and \textit{an} are used with singular countable nouns, and show that you are talking about just 1 person or thing.

%
\textit{Another} is used with singular countable nouns and \textit{other} with plural countable nouns, but only after 1 or more of the same type of person or thing has been mentioned.

%
\textit{Any} can be used with singular and plural countable nouns to talk about 1 or more people or things.

You use \textit{enough, few, many, more, most, several}, and \textit{some} with plural countable nouns to show that you are talking about a number of people or things.

Each of these determiners indicates a different set or group within the total number.

For more information about their meanings, see the section beginning at paragraph \textbf{1.236}.

%
\textit{All, both, each, either}, and \textit{every} show that you are talking about the total number of people or things involved.

\textit{Both} and \textit{either} specify that only 2 people or things are involved.

\textit{Both} is used with a plural noun, and \textit{either} with a singular noun.

\textit{All, each}, and \textit{every} usually show that there are more than 2.

\textit{All} is used with a plural noun, and \textit{each} and \textit{every} with a singular noun.

%
\textit{No} and \textit{neither} refer to the total number of things involved in negative statements.

\textit{No} is used with singular or plural nouns, and \textit{neither} only with singular nouns.

\textit{No} and \textit{neither} are covered in the section on \textbf{negative words} in Chap. 5.

%
For more information about \textbf{countable nouns}, see paragraphs \textbf{1.15--1.22}.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.225} For uncountable nouns, \textit{any, enough, little, more, most, much}, and \textit{some} are used to talk about a quantity of something.

\textit{No} and \textit{all} indicate the total quantity of it.

%
For more information about \textbf{uncountable nouns}, see paragraphs \textbf{1.23--1.33}.

%
\textbf{[!] Be careful.}

%
\noindent\fbox{1.226} \textit{A, an, another, both, each, either, every, few, many, neither}, and \textit{several} are not usually used with uncountable nouns.

%
\noindent\fbox{1.227} When you are referring to things or people in an indefinite way, you can sometimes use a noun without a determiner.
\begin{example}
    $\ldots$ raising \underline{money} from \underline{industry}, \underline{government}, and \underline{trusts}.
    
    \underline{Permission} should be asked before \underline{visitors} are invited.
\end{example}
\textbf{Uncountable nouns} are usually used without a determiner.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Health} and \underline{education} are matters that most voters feel strongly about.
    
    \underline{Wealth}, like \underline{power}, tends to corrupt.
\end{example}
\textbf{Plural nouns} are used without a determiner when you are referring to all the people or things of a particular kind.
\begin{example}
    \underline{Dogs} need a regular balanced diet, not just meat.
    
    Are there any jobs that \underline{men} can do that \underline{women} can't?
\end{example}
Plural nouns can also be used without a determiner to talk about an unspecified number of things.
\begin{example}
    Teachers should read \underline{stories} to children.
    
    Cats and dogs get \underline{fleas}.
\end{example}
For more information about plural nouns, see paragraphs \textbf{1.41--1.46}.
