\chapter{\href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz_Association}{Wikipedia/Leibniz Association}}

The \textit{Leibniz Association} (German: \textit{Leibniz-Gemeinschaft} or \textit{Wissenschaftsgemeinschaft Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz}) is a union of German non-university research institutes from various disciplines.

%
As of 2020, 96 non-university research institutes and service institutions for science are part of the Leibniz-Gemeinschaft.

The fields range from natural science, engineering, and ecology, to economics, other social sciences, \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spatial_science}{spatial science}, and humanities.

The Leibniz Institutes work in an interdisciplinary fashion, and connect basic and applied science.

They cooperate with universities, industry, and other partners in different parts of the world.

Taken together, the Leibniz Institutes employ 20,000 people and have a budget of €1.9 billion.[``About the Leibniz Association''. \url{www.leibniz-gemeinschaft.de}.]

Leibniz Institutes are funded publicly to equal parts by the federal government and the Federal states (Bundesländer).

%
Every Leibniz institution is evaluated by the Leibniz Senate regularly, at a minimum of once every 7 years.

The evaluation is used as a benchmark of quality w.r.t. the work and research carried out by the institutes.[``Leibniz Gemeinschaft: About us/Evaluation''. \url{www.leibniz-gemeinschaft.de}. Retrieved 2019-03-17.]

\section{History}
The Leibniz-Gemeinschaft is named after the German philosopher, mathematician, scientist, and inventor \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz}{Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz} (1646-1716).

%
The Leibniz Association evolved from the ``Blaue Liste'' (blue list) in former Western Germany and research institutions of the German Academy of Sciences at Berlin of the former \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Democratic_Republic}{DDR}, whose research capability was deemed worth keeping after an evaluation by the German \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wissenschaftsrat}{Wissenschaftsrat}.

The name `Blaue Liste' for a German model for funding science has been retired, and traces back to the color of a dossier.

%
The Leibniz Association's headquarter is located in \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin}{Berlin} and there is an EU bureau in Brussels.

Since 2014, the engineer Matthias Kleiner has been president of the Leibniz Association, with Christiane Neumann acting as secretary general.

\textbf{Weierstrass Institute.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Abbreviation.} WIAS
    \item \textbf{Predecessor.} Karl Weierstrass Institute for Mathematics of the GDR's Academy of Science.
    \item \textbf{Formation.} 1992.
    \item \textbf{Type.} Research Institute.
    \item \textbf{Purpose.} Pure and applied mathematical research.
    \item \textbf{Location.} Berlin.
    \item \textbf{Director.} Michael Hintermüller
    \item \textbf{Parent organization.} Forschungsverbund Berlin e.V.

    \item \textbf{Website.} \href{http://www.wias-berlin.de/index.jsp?lang=1}{www.wias-berlin.de}
\end{itemize}
The \textit{Weierstrass Institute}, formally the \textit{Weierstrass Institute for Applied Analysis and Stochastics} (WIAS), is a part of the Forschungsverbund Berlin e.V.[``Weierstrass Institute for Applied Analysis and Stochastics (WIAS), Leibniz Institute in Forschungsverbund Berlin e.V. - Forschungsverbund Berlin''. www.fv-berlin.de. Retrieved Feb 25, 2016.] and a member of the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz_Association}{Leibniz Association}.[``Leibniz Gemeinschaft: Institutes \& Museums/WIAS''. \url{www.leibniz-gemeinschaft.de}. Retrieved Feb 25, 2016.]

Based in Berlin's district Mitte, the institute's research activities involve \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics}{applied} and \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_mathematics}{pure mathematics}.

%
Since Feb 2011, the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Mathematical_Union}{International Mathematical Union} (IMU) Secretariat has been located in Berlin at WIAS.[``International Mathematical Union (IMU): Secretariat''. \url{www.mathunion.org}. Retrieved Feb 25, 2016.]

\section{History}
The institute developed from the ``Karl Weierstrass Institute for Mathematics'' of the former \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GDR}{GDR}'s \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Academy_of_Sciences_at_Berlin}{Academy of Sciences at Berlin}.

Following a recommendation of the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Council_of_Science_and_Humanities}{German Council of Science and Humanities}, the institute was established on Jan 1, 1992.

%
The institute is named after the Berlin mathematician \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Weierstra%C3%9F}{Karl Weierstraß} (1815-1897), who was well known for his profound processing of analysis.

\section{Tasks}
The main task of WIAS is the implementation of project-oriented research in \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics}{applied mathematics}, especially in applied \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analysis}{analysis} and \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastics}{stochastics}.

The research activity is oriented on specific application situations and is confirmed by cooperations with scientific institutions and the economy.

It includes the entire spectrum of the solution, from \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model}{mathematical modeling} to mathematical-theoretical analysis of models, up to the development of \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm}{algorithms} and numerical simulation of technological processes.

%
Research at WIAS focuses on the following application areas:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanoelectronics}{Nano}- and \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optoelectronics}{Optoelectronics},
    \item \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_optimization}{Optimization} and \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_theory}{control} in \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technology}{technology} and economy,
    \item Materials modeling,
    \item Flow and transport,
    \item \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_transformation}{Conversion}, \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_storage}{storage} and \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_power_distribution}{distribution} of \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy}{energy},
    \item Random phenomena in \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature}{nature} and \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy}{economy}.
    
    [``Leibniz Gemeinschaft: Institutes \& Museums/WIAS''. \url{www.leibniz-gemeinschaft.de}. Retrieved Feb 25, 2016.]
\end{itemize}
Within these application areas, questions are investigated, that play a central role in the development of key technologies (e.g. \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materials_science}{materials science}, \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manufacturing_engineering}{manufacturing engineering}, \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomedical_engineering}{biomedical engineering}), as well as applications in the economy (e.g. \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finance}{finances}).

\chapter{\href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Academy_of_Sciences_at_Berlin}{Wikipedia/German Academy of Sciences at Berlin}}

\textsf{Seat of the academy at the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gendarmenmarkt}{Gendarmenmarkt}.}

The \textit{German Academy of Sciences at Berlin}, \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language}{German}: \textit{Deutsche Akademie der Wissenschaften zu Berlin} (\textit{DAW}), in 1972 renamed the \textit{Academy of Sciences of the GDR (Akademie der Wissenschaften der DDR} (\textit{AdW})), was the most eminent \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Research_institute}{research institution} of \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Germany}{East Germany} (German Democratic Republic, GDR).

%
The academy was established in 1946 in an attempt to continue the tradition of the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences}{Prussian Academy of Sciences} and the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brandenburg_Society_of_Sciences}{Brandenburg Society of Sciences}, founded in 1700 by \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz}{Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz}.[Mark Walker (11 November 2013). \textit{Nazi Science: Myth, Truth, and the German Atomic Bomb}. Springer. pp. 1-. ISBN 978-1-4899-6074-0.]

The academy was a \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Learned_society}{Learned society} (scholarship society), in which awarded membership via election constituted scientific recognition.

Unlike other academies of science, the DAW was also the host organization of a scientific community of non-academic research institutes.[``\textit{Branchen-Fernsprechbuch für die Hauptstadt der Deutschen Demokratischen Republik Berlin Issue 1988 - Academic institutions}''. Zentral- und Landesbibliothek Berlin. Retrieved May 30, 2020.]

%
Upon \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_reunification}{German reunification}, the Academy's Learned society was dissociated from its research institutes and any other affiliates and eventually dissolved in 1992.

Activities of the AdW's members and college have since 1993 been continued by the newly established \textit{Leibniz Scientific Society} (Leibniz-Sozietät der Wissenschaften).

The AdW's pending and unfinished research projects and holdings were forwarded to and are carried out by the 1992 established \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin-Brandenburg_Academy_of_Sciences_and_Humanities}{Berlin-Brandenburg Academy of Sciences and Humanities}.

The academy's countless institutes were dissolved on Dec 31, 1991 and partially reorganized into other organizations such as the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leibniz_Association}{Leibniz Association}, the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_Association_of_German_Research_Centres}{Helmholtz Association of German Research Centres}, the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max\_Planck\_Society}{Max Planck Society} and the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraunhofer_Society}{Fraunhofer Society}.

A number of minor institutes and associated projects have been preserved and were transferred to other institutions such as the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Archaeological_Institute}{German Archaeological Institute}.

\section{DAW (1946--1972)}
The German Academy of Sciences at Berlin was the successor to the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brandenburg_Society_of_Sciences}{Brandenburg Society of Sciences}, that had been founded by \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz}{Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz} in the year 1700.

After the end of World War II, it was re-established upon the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Military_Administration_in_Germany}{SMAD} Order No. 187 of Jul 1, 1946, Leibniz's 300th birthday.

The Academy was to become the most eminent scientific institution in Germany.

Reorganization was greatly influenced by the ideas of the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_Soviet_Union}{Academy of Sciences of the Soviet Union}.[Eckart Förtsch; Hubert Laitko; Reinhard Siegmund-Schultze (1999). \textit{Science Under Socialism: East Germany in Comparative Perspective}. Harvard University Press. pp. 140--. ISBN 978-0-674-79477-1.]

%
To facilitate publishing, the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akademie_Verlag}{Akademie Verlag} was founded in 1946.

The 250th anniversary in 1950 was boycotted by West Germany in protest of the overwhelming influence of the East German authorities.

The \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Unity_Party_of_Germany}{Socialist Unity Party of East Germany} had embraced the \textit{2-nation} doctrine and increasingly enforced its will upon the electorate to have mostly East Germans elected to the academy in the following decades.[Dieter Hoffmann. ``\textit{The Divided Centennial: The 1958 Max Planck Celebration(s) in Berlin}''. University of Chicago. Retrieved May 30, 2020.][Patrick Manning; Mat Savelli (29 June 2018). \textit{Global Transformations in the Life Sciences}, 1945--1980. University of Pittsburgh Press. pp. 112--. ISBN 978-0-8229-8605-8.][Heinrich August Winkler (11 October 2007). \textit{Germany: The Long Road West: Volume 2: 1933-1990}. OUP Oxford. pp. 270–. ISBN 978-0-19-150061-9.]

\section{AdW (1972--1989)}
The institution became the most eminent \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academy}{academy} of the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Germany}{German Democratic Republic}, and was accordingly renamed \textit{Academy of Sciences of the GDR} (Akademie der Wissenschaften der DDR - AdW) in 1972, once the division of Germany was accepted as the state of affairs.

In the 1980s, the AdW itself had grown to accommodate over 200 members, including around 2 dozen West German scientists.

The academy coordinated research of 59 institutes that employed 22,000 persons.

\section{Unwinding and Re-establishments (1989--1993)}
Following the fall of the Berlin wall, academy members called for a reform of the academy, rejecting the leading role of the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialist_Unity_Party_of_Germany}{Socialist Unity Party of Germany}.[Ralf Rytlewski (1 July 2013). \textit{Politik und Gesellschaft in sozialistischen Ländern: Ergebnisse und Probleme der Sozialistischen Länder-Forschung. Springer-Verlag}. pp. 65--. ISBN 978-3-663-11066-8.]

%
On Jun 27, 1990, the new GDR government reorganized the academy, turning it into a public institution.

Until late 1991, the former AdW institutes were separated from the academy, evaluated, and either dissolved or assigned to different organizations, mainly the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz_Scientific_Community}{Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz Scientific Community}.

As the states of Berlin and Brandenburg considered a continuation of the academy as improper due to its role in the GDR, the academy, which had then about 400 members, was disbanded and the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin-Brandenburg_Academy_of_Sciences_and_Humanities}{Berlin-Brandenburg Academy of Sciences and Humanities} was established in 1993.[``\textit{A future-orientated science institution of more than 300 years of history}''. BBAW. Retrieved May 30, 2020.]

%
On Apr 15, 1993, 60 of the former academy members created the private organization \textit{Leibniz-Sozietät} which claims to represent 300 years of continuous academic tradition.

It has now over 300 members, of which most were elected since 1994.[``\textit{Von der Kurfürstlich Brandenburgischen Sozietät der Wissenschaften zur Leibniz-Sozietät der Wissenschaften zu Berlin}''. Leibniz-Sozietät. Retrieved May 30, 2020.]

\chapter{\href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gendarmenmarkt}{Wikipedia/Gendarmenmarkt}}

\textsf{2008 panorama of the Gendarmenmarkt, showing the Konzerthaus, flanked by the German Church (left) and French Church (right).}

The \textit{Gendarmenmarkt} is a square in \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin}{Berlin} and the site of an architectural ensemble including the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konzerthaus_Berlin}{Berlin concert hall} and the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Cathedral}{French} and \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Cathedral}{German Churches}.

In the center of the square stands a \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schiller_Monument_(Berlin)}{monumental statue} of poet \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Schiller}{Friedrich Schiller}.

The square was created by \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Arnold_Nering}{Johann Arnold Nering} at the end of the 17th century as the Linden-Markt and reconstructed by \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Christian_Unger}{Georg Christian Unger} in 1773.

The Gendarmenmarkt is named after the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuirassier}{cuirassier} regiment \textit{Gens d'Armes}, which had stables at the square until 1773.

\textsf{Gendarmenmarkt around 1900.}

%
During \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II}{World War II}, most of the buildings were badly damaged or destroyed.

Today all of them have been restored.

\textsf{View of Gendarmenmarkt with the Konzerthaus to the right and the German Church in the back, as seen from the top of the French Church, 2011.}

\section{Origins}
Gendarmenmarkt was 1st built in 1688.

It was a marketplace and part of the city's Western expansion of Friedrichstadt, 1 of Berlin's emerging quarters.[``Architecture''. \url{www.konzerthaus.de}. Konzerthaus Berlin. Archived from the original on Oct 17, 2014. Retrieved Nov 25, 2014.]

\section{Franz\"osischer Dom}
The \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Cathedral}{French Church} (in German: \textit{Französischer Dom}, where \textit{Dom} refers to the ``dome'' and not to a cathedral.

Neither the French nor the German Church was ever the seat of a bishop. The terminology is a relic of francophone \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_the_Great}{Frederick the Great}, who was instrumental in enhancing the Gendarmenmarkt) is the older of the 2 churches, was built by the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huguenot}{Huguenot} community between 1701 and 1705.

It was modeled after the destroyed Huguenot church in \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saint-Maurice,_Val-de-Marne}{Charenton-Saint-Maurice}, France.

The tower and \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portico}{porticoes}, designed by \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_von_Gontard}{Carl von Gontard}, were added to the building in 1785.

The French Church has a viewing platform, a restaurant and a Huguenot museum. 

\textsf{Gendarmenmarkt at dusk.}

\section{Deutscher Dom}
\textsf{German Church and Concert Hall.}

The \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Cathedral}{German Church} (in German: \textit{Deutscher Dom}) is located to the south of the Gendarmenmarkt.

It has a \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal}{pentagonal} structure and was designed by \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Gr%C3%BCnberg}{Martin Grünberg} and built in 1708 by \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Simonetti}{Giovanni Simonetti}.
    
This church belonged to the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutheran}{Lutheran} community.[``\textit{Gendarmenmarkt - History of the Square}''. \url{www.gendarmenmarkt.de}. Retrieved Nov 25, 2014.]

It too was modified in 1785 by Carl von Gontard, who built the domed tower.

The German Church was completely destroyed by fire in 1945, during \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II}{World War II}.

After \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_reunification}{German reunification} it was rebuilt, finished in 1993 and re-opened in 1996 as a museum of German history.

\section{Konzerthaus}
The \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konzerthaus_Berlin}{Konzerthaus Berlin} is the most recent building on the Gendarmenmarkt.

It was built by \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Friedrich_Schinkel}{Karl Friedrich Schinkel} in 1821 as the \textit{Schauspielhaus}.

It was based on the ruins of the National Theatre, which had been destroyed by fire in 1817.

Parts of the building contain columns and some outside walls from the destroyed building.

Like the other buildings on the square, it was also badly damaged during World War II.

The reconstruction, finished in 1984, turned the theatre into a concert hall.

Today, it is the home of the \href{https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Konzerthausorchester_Berlin}{Konzerthausorchester Berlin}.
